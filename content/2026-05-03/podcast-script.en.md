**[Part 1: AI Commercialization Divergence]**

**A:** Right. Let's start with Nvidia. They just announced major investments in "physical AI"—that is, embodied intelligence—bringing AI from the digital world into the physical one.

**B:** This move has driven up stock prices for their robotics and automation partners in Asia. The market is essentially reassessing—when AI starts controlling physical devices, how should the entire supply chain's value be recalibrated?

**A:** Speaking of monetization pressure, Google has made a new move recently. They've confirmed they're adding ads to Gemini, though they say it's still in the preparation phase.

**B:** This actually sends a signal—the era of completely free AI chatbots might be coming to an end. Even a company like Google, with its massive advertising foundation, feels the need to use ad revenue to cover AI service costs. It shows the "burn cash for users" model is really feeling the strain.

**A:** The situation at Apple is more interesting. On one hand, the new CEO Ternus might change the cautious cash strategy of the Cook era, shifting toward more aggressive acquisitions; but on the other hand, they've tightened up on app reviews.

**B:** Right. Many AI startups working on "vibe coding" are complaining that Apple is holding up their reviews citing security risks. These apps use AI to analyze user emotions to assist with programming—they're actually quite innovative. Now it seems Apple's review policy has become a practical barrier to AI application innovation.

**[Part 2: Macro and Geopolitics]**

**A:** Stepping outside the tech world, on the macro data front, the upcoming U.S. employment report is worth watching. This is the first comprehensive employment data since the outbreak of the Iran war.

**B:** Right. The market wants to see whether the economy remains resilient under high interest rates and geopolitical conflict. This data will directly influence the Fed's upcoming rate decisions, so global traders are all watching closely.

**A:** There's movement in Asian markets too. Japan's Finance Minister once again refused to comment on whether they intervened in the foreign exchange market, sticking to that vague strategy in the face of sharp yen fluctuations.

**B:** The energy market is equally nuanced. OPEC just agreed to a symbolic production increase, but the UAE is pushing for long-term investment in the oil industry. See, oil producers are already divided on future demand assessments—some want to stabilize current prices, while others are already positioning for the transition.

**A:** Also, on May 9th, Russia's Victory Day, Putin will deliver an important speech and meet with Lukashenko and other national leaders.

**B:** Public statements at this juncture will directly impact market assessments of European security and energy supply stability. It's a critical window for judging geopolitical risk.

**[Part 3: Industry and Markets]**

**A:** Finally, let's look at the EV space. Cui Dongshu, Secretary-General of the China Passenger Car Association, predicts that by 2026, China's share of the global pure electric vehicle market will drop from 63% this year to 56%.

**B:** Although that's still over half, this downward trend shows overseas competition is intensifying. For Chinese automakers, they may no longer be able to rely solely on domestic market scale advantages—they'll need to depend more on technology and brand to go global.

**A:** Additionally, the Asian Development Bank just announced a major plan—to invest $70 billion by 2035 to support the "Pan-Asian Power Grid" and "Asia-Pacific Digital Highway."

**B:** This shows the Asia-Pacific region's long-term commitment to infrastructure connectivity—whether it's cross-border electricity trade or broadband access, these are solid, real demands.
