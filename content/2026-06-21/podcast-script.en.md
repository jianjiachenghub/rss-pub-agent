**A:** If you had to sum up today’s market vibe in two words, what would you pick?

**B:** Probably “tense” and “frenzied.” On one side, the Strait of Hormuz is actually closed to shipping, and oil markets are roiling. On the other side, SpaceX just pulled off a record IPO and is immediately turning around to issue $20 billion in bonds to bet on space-based computing. That disconnect feels pretty stark.

**A:** Let’s start with the tense side. Latest news from Iran: maritime traffic through the strait has dropped to zero. They’ve threatened blockades before, but this time it looks like they mean it.

**B:** One-fifth of global crude oil passes through there. Right now it’s not just about oil prices—tanker insurance costs are spiking, and actual transportation has stalled. That means even without a near-term military escalation, the “risk premium” on energy prices is going to stay elevated. It won’t come down easily in the short term.

**A:** This geopolitical uncertainty is happening at the worst possible time for the Fed. The core PCE metric they care about most is expected to show inflation bouncing back.

**B:** Right. The market was still guessing whether there’d be a rate cut in September. But once this data comes out, rate-cut expectations will definitely get pushed back again. Inflation is stickier than people thought, and that’s not good news for the pricing of global risk assets.

**A:** But the capital markets don’t seem to care about these macro headwinds at all. Look at SpaceX—fresh off a record IPO, and now it’s issuing $20 billion in debt to build AI data centers in orbit.

**B:** It’s basically an all-in leveraged play. Net debt is projected to exceed $400 billion by 2031. It’s a bet that the future AI computing battle won’t be on the ground, but in space. This could become the new valuation anchor for private space companies, but the risk is also extreme.

**A:** Speaking of AI capital frenzy, there’s another detail today. Anysphere, the parent company of the AI coding tool Cursor, was acquired—$60 billion valuation. Founded by four MIT students, and the first 50 employees are essentially set for life.

**B:** Annual revenue just crossed $1 billion. It shows AI coding might be the most commercially certain application-layer opportunity right now—way more concrete than those large foundation models still burning cash. That valuation level really echoes SpaceX’s crazy fundraising.

**A:** But it’s not all good news in AI. On the Manus front, early investors are planning to buy back the company from Meta for $2 billion, because China’s antitrust regulators blocked the previous deal.

**B:** That’s a clear signal. Premium AI assets are now being forced to stay domestic. Regulators are taking a hard line on cross-border M&A. Next, we’ll see if they can restructure as a joint-venture entity and list in Hong Kong. That’ll affect the capital strategy for a lot of similar companies.

**A:** Turning to domestic news, Tencent made a small move that might quietly change habits. WeChat is soft-launching an AI assistant called “Xiaowei” in limited beta.

**B:** The key is, it’s not just for chatting. It can directly operate native functions inside WeChat—sending messages, making calls, that sort of thing. If it rolls out to everyone, this becomes the biggest AI Agent entry point in China, and the logic of app distribution might have to be rewritten.

**A:** Finally, a welcome bit of news that actually touches everyday life. Hainan Airlines changed its refund rules: if the airline causes your flight to be moved to an earlier time, you can get a full refund no matter how far in advance it is.

**B:** This actually came from a college student who sued and won. The flight was moved up by just ten minutes. I think this matters. It shows consumer rights protection is shifting from “formal compliance” to “substantive fairness.” In times of massive change, this kind of subtle rule evolution might protect ordinary people’s day-to-day experience better than any grand narrative.

**A:** We covered a lot of ground today—from a strait closure to plane ticket refunds. But the common thread might be that old rules are unraveling faster, whether in geopolitical order or commercial terms.

**B:** Exactly. In this kind of environment, staying sensitive to concrete details is more useful than guessing at abstract trends. See you next time.
