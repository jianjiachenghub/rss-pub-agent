Personal Daily Podcast Script

**Hosts:** A / B  
**Total Duration:** Approx. 5 minutes  

---

**A:** We're on. Global markets are pretty busy today—from the Strait of Hormuz in the Middle East to Alibaba's new model release, it feels like geopolitics and tech commercialization are pulling in different directions at the same time.

**B:** Right, I've sorted through it, and today's main theme is really "expectation rebalancing." On one side, geopolitical risks and policy uncertainty are stirring up energy and forex markets; on the other, AI is moving from the lab toward scaled commercial deployment. Should we start with the macro side?

**A:** Sure. Let's look at the UK first. Bank of England Governor Pill sent a hawkish signal, hinting they might need to raise rates to tackle inflation.

**B:** That's a quick shift in the wind. Just earlier, markets were guessing whether the UK would follow the Fed's pivot toward easing; now rate hike expectations are back on the table.

**A:** Mm, suggests UK inflation might be stickier than thought. Major central banks' monetary policies are clearly diverging now—each marching to their own beat.

**B:** Speaking of divergence, there's new movement in the Middle East too. On optimism around peace agreements, crude prices tanked today, while US Treasury yields actually moved higher.

**A:** The transmission logic here is pretty clear—peace expectations lowered inflation risk premiums and reduced the need for safe-haven assets, so oil falls and bond prices rise.

**B:** Right, markets are repricing geopolitical risk. But interestingly, almost simultaneously, Iran announced that shipping through the Strait of Hormuz has fully resumed. Logically, with the risk lifted, gold should have fallen, but instead it hit a one-month high.

**A:** That's a bit intriguing. Investors probably figure it's easy to say traffic is flowing, but if fighting breaks out, the strait could be blocked just as quickly. This uncertainty is actually driving safe-haven demand for gold. After all, actual control of the strait is still a question mark.

**B:** Exactly. Eliminating risk and perceiving risk are two different things.

**A:** Okay, let's switch to the tech channel and look at some concrete progress. Waymo's co-CEO disclosed details of their large-scale commercial operations—completing hundreds of thousands of fully driverless rides per week.

**B:** That volume really shows Level 4 autonomous driving is starting to actually work at scale. They specifically emphasized using multi-sensor fusion plus simulation training—not the simple pure-vision approach.

**A:** Right, this actually sets a technical benchmark for the industry. In the face of engineering reality—especially in extreme weather or complex road conditions—the pure-vision approach probably still needs multi-sensor backup to scale.

**B:** Looking domestically now. Alibaba open-sourced Qwen3.6-35B-A3B using a MoE architecture—35 billion total parameters, but only activating 3 billion per inference.

**A:** It's a clever design—essentially achieving large-model performance with minimal compute, bringing inference costs down significantly. The competition has shifted from who has more parameters to who's more efficient.

**B:** Much friendlier for SMEs and developers. Speaking of cost pressures, there were two big corporate stories today. Netflix issued weak earnings guidance, the stock tanked, and co-founder Hastings announced he's stepping down.

**A:** That's a double whammy. Weak earnings you could chalk up to industry cycles, but the founder leaving at this moment—markets can't help but question whether the company's long-term direction is changing. The high-growth era for streaming might really be over.

**B:** Nike is facing similar growing pains in its transition. The stock keeps falling, and Wall Street is clearly losing patience with their recovery plan.

**A:** The balance between consumer brand premium pricing and rational consumption is genuinely hard to strike right now. And macro-wise, even India—an engine of growth—is showing signals of potential economic slowdown.

**B:** If India slows down too, the pressure on global demand becomes even heavier. Finally, a very specific manufacturing story—US jet ski manufacturers are caught in a "reshoring" dilemma because of tariff policy uncertainty.

**A:** This is the gap between policy ideals and economic reality. Tariffs bouncing up and down means companies can't make long-term plans at all—they'd rather wait and see than risk investing in new plants. This uncertainty hurts more than high tariffs themselves.

**B:** Exactly. That wraps up today's stories—from the Bank of England's policy pivot, to Middle East maneuvering, to AI's engineering deployment—every layer is adjusting expectations.

**A:** Right, markets are essentially finding new equilibrium points through constant digestion. See you tomorrow.
