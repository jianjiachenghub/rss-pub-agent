**Podcast Script: Personal Daily Report**

**Total Duration: Approx. 4 minutes 50 seconds**

---

**[Opening]**

**A:** There's been a notable shift in the market lately—the conversation around AI seems to be moving from "grand concepts" to "crunching the numbers." Whether it's power companies committing to tens of billions in capex or the iteration of memory chip technology, it's clear AI is transitioning from the cloud into tangible infrastructure.

**B:** Exactly, and today's threads are pretty clear: macro conditions are fluctuating, physical constraints are emerging, and product forms are evolving. Let's follow this narrative today and pick out a few noteworthy changes worth watching.

---

**[Part One: Macro and Market Sentiment]**

**A:** First, a quick scan of the big picture. News of a potential US-Iran deal sparked a rally in global bond markets, while oil prices dropped in response.

**B:** When geopolitical tensions ease, safe-haven demand cools, and capital naturally flows toward risk assets. But on the other hand, uncertainty around the Fed remains.

**A:** Yeah, despite the recent rebound in US stocks, expectations for rate cuts are actually diminishing. The economic data has remained resilient, inflation is sticky, and Fed officials have taken a hawkish stance. The market is reassessing the interest rate path, which remains a potential headwind for high-valuation tech stocks.

---

**[Part Two: AI's Physical Layer—Energy and Storage]**

**B:** Speaking of the underpinnings of tech stocks, we need to talk about AI's "physical limits." It used to be about chips being the bottleneck; now we're realizing electricity is a major issue too.

**A:** Right. US power giant AEP just raised its five-year capital investment plan to $78 billion, up 8%, specifically to address surging power demand from data centers. This reminds us that AI's expansion rate depends not just on GPU production capacity, but also on whether the grid can handle it. Energy infrastructure has become a new investment theme.

**B:** The memory chip space is buzzing too. Samsung Electronics just surpassed the trillion-dollar market cap mark, driven mainly by robust AI demand for High Bandwidth Memory (HBM). Moreover, Samsung, SK Hynix, and Micron have officially kicked off R&D for DDR6, which could be twice as fast as DDR5.

**A:** This shows memory is replacing GPUs as the market's valuation anchor in the near term. Look at the Nasdaq and S&P 500 hitting new highs—it's actually Intel and other memory manufacturers leading the charge. Capital is clearly flowing from AI infrastructure toward the broader semiconductor cycle recovery.

---

**[Part Three: Product Form and Ecosystem Landscape]**

**B:** While hardware is ratcheting up, the software side is undergoing a critical evolutionary leap. Meta is developing what they're calling "autonomous" AI assistants—not the kind of chatbot where you ask one question and get one answer, but agents that can plan and execute complex multi-step tasks on their own.

**A:** This is a crucial shift from passive response to proactive execution, potentially reconstructing how users interact with the digital world. But on the other hand, Apple's approach is interesting—Bloomberg says iOS 27 plans to let users choose competitor AI models for various features, rather than forcing them to use Apple's own products.

**B:** Open-source versus closed ecosystem—both paths are being pursued. Domestically, DeepSeek's valuation has approached $45 billion, with the National Big Fund leading its first external funding round. This means state-level capital has officially entered the game, potentially rewriting the competitive landscape for AI startups.

---

**[Part Four: Costs, Supply Chain, and Regulation]**

**A:** But behind the excitement lies a sobering reality: costs are climbing. Rising memory chip prices are pushing up training and inference costs, raising the profitability threshold for AI commercialization. And on the Nvidia front, 90% of production costs rely on Asian supply chains—that's a tangible geopolitical risk sitting right there.

**B:** Regulation is tightening in parallel. Google, Microsoft, and xAI have agreed to share early AI models with the US government, and safety evaluation frameworks are taking shape. Meanwhile, Chinese regulators vetoed Meta's acquisition of Manus, making the boundaries between technological sovereignty and antitrust increasingly clear.

---

**[Closing]**

**A:** All in all, the AI industry is going through a transition from "can we do it" to "does it make economic sense." Whether it's power investments, memory iterations, or cost controls, the sector is entering a more pragmatic phase.

**B:** Right. Going forward, the key question is whether these massive infrastructure investments can translate into real application returns. Those valuations driven by storytelling will ultimately have to face the test of cash flow. We'll keep watching.

**A:** Alright, that's it for today.
