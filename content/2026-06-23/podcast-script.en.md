**Podcast Script: Personal Daily**

**Duration:** Approx. 4 min 50 sec  
**Hosts:** Host A (Analytical Perspective), Host B (Observational Perspective)

---

**A:** Look at the market today. Tech stocks got crushed, logging their biggest single-day drop since March. But on the flip side, the AI track is still hoovering up cash—hundred-billion-dollar funds are springing up overnight. This kind of polarized, fire-and-ice situation actually shows AI is moving from "concept hype" into a "reality shock" phase.

**B:** Right, and the word "reality" hits especially hard today. Oracle just confirmed that in the past twelve months it laid off 21,000 people, and said it outright—some of those roles were replaced by AI. This isn't some vague speak about "optimizing structure." It's the first time a traditional software giant has written "AI replacement" directly into its layoff reasoning.

**A:** Twenty-one thousand... that figure is sobering. We used to talk about AI's impact on jobs like it was a distant risk a few years out. Now it's hard data in an earnings report. But the irony is, right as this news dropped, global tech stocks fell anyway. The market is reassessing—if AI really can cut costs this much, maybe those sky-high valuations that were purely propped up by stories need to have some foam squeezed out of them.

**B:** I'd call it a healthy correction. Money is shifting from "who has the best narrative" to "who can actually deliver." And two product moves today were fascinating. One, Amazon is now selling its homegrown Trainium chips directly to the outside world, not just renting them through cloud services. Two, Anthropic's Claude launched a Tag feature. You can @ it in Slack, assign it to follow up on projects like a colleague, and it remembers the context.

**A:** Reading these two signals together, AI is transforming from a "tool" into "infrastructure" and a "team member." Amazon challenging Nvidia's hardware ecosystem, Claude challenging traditional SaaS workflows—both are fighting for the next-gen ecosystem slot. There's a similar landing attempt back home too. ByteDance's Doubao app is beta-testing a ride-hailing feature. Users can book a car through a direct voice conversation, with Caocao Chuxing handling the orders.

**B:** The path is crystal clear: AI assistants aren't just doing Q&A anymore; they want to become the aggregation gateway for transactional services. From "there's a car nearby" to "I've booked it and completed payment for you"—once that step is crossed, the business model is truly a closed loop. But speaking of ecosystem games, the most subtle move today might be Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella's statement. He suddenly came out publicly calling to break the monopoly of AI giants, advocating for cheap models and user choice.

**A:** Sounds contradictory, doesn't it? Microsoft and OpenAI are tied at the hip, after all. But read between the lines: this is actually aimed at Google and Anthropic's closed-source advantage. By pushing a multi-model ecosystem and weakening the lock-in of its rivals, Microsoft ultimately strengthens its own position as an open platform. It's a more sophisticated competitive play.

**B:** Of course, AI isn't all business games. Intelligence agencies from the Five Eyes alliance issued a joint warning today, saying AI-driven cyberattacks and deepfakes could be successfully pulled off "within months." That's the other side of the coin—the more powerful the technology, the larger the risk exposure. When AI truly embeds itself in critical infrastructure like finance and energy, security protection has to upgrade in lockstep. Some institutions, like BBVA, are even starting to use financial instruments to transfer AI risk onto investors.

**A:** So today's theme is crystal clear: AI is passing through the peak of the "inflated expectations" phase and entering a real stage of commercial restructuring—some people lose jobs, some lose money, some are rewriting the rules, and others are starting to put a price on risk.

**B:** Right. Letting some air out of the bubble isn't a bad thing. The key is seeing who can actually turn the technology into a sustainable business. We'll keep watching tomorrow.
