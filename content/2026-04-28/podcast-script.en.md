**[00:45-01:25] OpenAI and Microsoft Decoupling**

**B:** First, let's talk about the Microsoft situation. OpenAI terminated that previous exclusive agreement, so Microsoft will no longer be the sole supplier for certain AI technologies. The logic is obvious—they don't want to put all their eggs in one basket.

**A:** This definitely gives them much more strategic flexibility. They can partner with more cloud providers and have more financing options. But for Microsoft, this is a serious blow. After all, Azure was previously the main gateway for OpenAI, and now other platforms have a shot at that business.

**B:** The market landscape is definitely going to shift. We might see OpenAI's technology showing up on Google Cloud or AWS. That's good news for enterprise clients—more choices.

**[01:25-02:00] Revenue Pressure and Market Reaction**

**A:** But independence comes at a cost. Bloomberg reported that OpenAI's sales revenue missed market expectations, which directly triggered investor concerns about profitability across the AI industry. Tech stocks dipped across the board today.

**B:** This signal is crucial. Before, everyone was pouring money into building data centers and buying chips. Now the market is asking: when does this money actually come back? If the leader, OpenAI, can't meet expectations, the pressure on other companies will only get worse.

**A:** So you see, the pullback in US stocks from their highs isn't just an OpenAI problem—it's a repricing of the entire market's view on AI commercialization prospects.

---

**[02:00-02:40] US-China Tech Rivalry: The Manus Acquisition**

**B:** Shifting our view back home, there was another major story today—Chinese regulators blocked Meta's proposed $2 billion acquisition of AI company Manus, demanding that Meta withdraw the deal entirely.

**A:** This intervention was decisive, clearly aimed at preventing core AI technology and data assets from flowing overseas. And interestingly, once the news broke, apparently a Chinese billionaire immediately started restructuring his own AI startup's architecture to respond to this kind of geopolitical risk.

**B:** Right. This signals that China is tightening its review of cross-border tech M&A, especially deals involving algorithms and underlying technology. Going forward, such acquisitions will face much higher hurdles to clear.

---

**[02:40-03:20] Supply Chain Crisis: From Chips to Water**

**A:** Speaking of technology deployment, there's a bottleneck more urgent than algorithms right now—the hardware supply chain. *The Economist* reported that the AI industry is facing a chip shortage, with specialized chipsets unable to keep up with the demands of model production.

**B:** But paradoxically, upstream manufacturers are making a killing. Suppliers to NVIDIA, for instance, are seeing sales surge thanks to AI demand. This shows money is indeed flowing into the infrastructure layer, but production capacity simply can't ramp up fast enough.

**A:** There's also a more realistic constraint—water. Bloomberg reported that AI data center expansion in Arizona is under serious threat due to the Colorado River water crisis. After all, cooling requires massive amounts of water, creating a hard physical constraint.

**B:** So AI development is now limited by electricity, water, and chips—these most basic resources have become the bottlenecks.

---

**[03:20-03:55] Regulatory Implementation: From Brussels to Beijing**

**A:** There were also moves on the regulatory front today. China's Ministry of Industry and Information Technology summoned platforms like CapCut and Jimeng AI because they failed to label AI-generated content as required, violating the Interim Measures for the Management of Generative Artificial Intelligence Services.

**B:** This marks China's AI regulation truly moving from the legislative phase into the enforcement phase. Going forward, all AIGC products must treat content identification as a standard feature. Operating costs will definitely rise, but this is the inevitable path to compliance.

**A:** The EU wasn't idle either. They demanded Google open its Android system to AI competitors to prevent it from using its mobile dominance to monopolize AI entry points. The regulatory philosophies in the East and West differ, but the target is the same—preventing giant monopolies.

---

**[03:55-04:35] New Trends: AI Agents and Engineering Paradigms**

**B:** Finally, let's touch on a few trends worth watching. The Collison brothers from Stripe mentioned "Agent Commerce" in a podcast—where AI agents autonomously complete commercial transactions. They see this as the future direction of software.

**A:** Right. Also, Eli Lilly signed a deal with Profluent worth up to $2.25 billion to use AI for designing new drugs. This shows the AI pharma business model is truly being recognized by mainstream pharmaceutical companies—it's no longer in the experimental phase.

**B:** Plus, there's something new in software engineering. Martin Fowler proposed SPDD—Structured Prompt-Driven Development—aiming to transform AI programming from a personal tool into a standard team collaboration process.

**A:** All of these point in the same direction: AI is moving from being a "toy" to a "production tool," but only if you solve those supply chain, regulatory, and profitability issues we mentioned today.
