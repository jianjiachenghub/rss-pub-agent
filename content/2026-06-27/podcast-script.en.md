A: The main thread most worth watching today really comes down to one word: constraints. AI model releases are being held back by government safety reviews, AI trades are being tested by valuations and leverage in the market, and AI infrastructure is being constrained by power and materials.

B: Right. So today is not simply about “models getting stronger again,” but about the AI boom entering a more realistic phase: who can launch, who can use it, how costs are calculated, and where the energy comes from will all shape the industry’s pace from here.

A: Let’s start with OpenAI. GPT-5.6 has already begun pre-release, but access is restricted to a small number of trusted partners. The reason is safety concerns from the U.S. government. OpenAI has also stressed that White House review of AI products should not become a long-term norm.

B: The key point here is that the release threshold for top-tier models has changed. In the past, people focused more on technical capability and commercial timing. Now, government safety review could lengthen the API rollout cycle. For enterprises, whether they can get access immediately is not just a budget question; it also depends on whether they are on the “trusted partner” list.

A: At the same time, GPT-5.6’s product tiers are also worth watching. OpenAI has divided it into three levels: Sol, Terra, and Luna: flagship, balanced everyday use, and fast low-cost. On pricing, the flagship version has not clearly gone up, while the lightweight version has sharply lowered call costs.

B: This will change the cost equation for AI applications. Enterprises won’t necessarily use the flagship model for every task. Instead, they’ll split complex reasoning, daily office work, and high-concurrency calls into different configurations. Model competition is also shifting from “who is strongest” to “who can balance cost and performance across different scenarios.”

A: Regulation is not only affecting OpenAI. Anthropic is also negotiating with the U.S. government, hoping to lift restrictions on its top-tier models. Meanwhile, prediction market Polymarket is facing a broader investigation from the CFTC.

B: This shows that regulatory boundaries are being redrawn. AI model companies need to build safety commitments into their release processes, and prediction markets may also be brought further under derivatives regulation. In the future, whether many products can launch will not be decided only by product managers and engineering teams; it will also depend on the pace of compliance.

A: On the market side, AI trades are starting to come under pressure. This week’s pullback in tech stocks caused Wall Street’s crowded AI trade to start reversing, and Bloomberg mentioned that a roughly $270 billion speculative machine is beginning to expose risks.

B: This does not mean the AI thesis has disappeared. It means capital is starting to test whether valuations can match growth. Once price declines trigger deleveraging, volatility gets amplified. A similar situation is also appearing in crypto ETFs. Institutional channels that were originally expected to stabilize Bitcoin may instead become fast redemption exits when risk appetite weakens.

A: Now look at infrastructure. AI’s bottleneck is expanding from chips to electricity. KKR agreed to acquire EDF’s renewable power assets in the U.S. and Canada, while utility executives also warned that the U.S. could face blackout risks because of power shortages.

B: Capital is already moving early to lock in power generation assets. AI data centers do not run just because they have GPUs. Long-term control over electricity will become part of the competition. The IMF also warned that the wealth effect brought by AI could push up demand-side inflation, which would affect central banks’ room to cut rates.

A: On the technical side, there is also an engineering-heavy but very important development: DeepSeek has deployed the DSpark speculative decoding framework in V4 Flash and Pro traffic, saying inference speed improved by about 57% to 85%, and it has open-sourced DeepSpec.

B: The significance of this kind of optimization is that it does not change the model’s output, but allows the same compute to handle more requests. For agents, developer tools, and real-time interaction products, lower response latency means a better experience, and it also directly affects service costs.

A: On the supply chain side, Nvidia was reported to have visited Ganzhou in Jiangxi multiple times to look for high-performance neodymium iron boron permanent magnet materials. Ganzhou concentrates a large share of China’s medium and heavy rare earth resources.

B: This reminds us that embodied intelligence and robotics are not just about chips and models. They also depend on joint motors, materials, torque density, and battery life. Rare earth supply will become a strategic variable in the robotics supply chain.

A: Finally, let’s look at China and consumer tech. DeepSeek released hiring notices, planning to at least double the size of every department. On Apple’s side, Ming-Chi Kuo said the base iPhone in 2027 may increase memory to 9GB to support deeper on-device AI.

B: Put these two together, and it means AI is moving from labs and cloud models into organizational expansion and hardware configuration. Domestic model companies are accelerating team building, while phone makers are raising the threshold for on-device capability. Next, AI competition will look more like a long-term operational race: models, costs, compliance, power supply, talent, and endpoints all have to be calculated together.
