A: The main thread worth watching today is that “AI capability” is being repriced. In the past, people mainly looked at whether the models were powerful enough and whether there were enough chips. Now they also have to look at three things: whether governments allow it to be used, whether the power grid can support it, and whether capital is still willing to pay high valuations.

B: Right. OpenAI’s GPT-5.6 is a typical signal. It has been opened to specific users vetted by the U.S. government, but it has not been fully released to everyone. Anthropic’s Mythos 5 is similar: from restrictions to partial restoration of access, what sits behind it is safety review and customer eligibility shaping model distribution.

A: This means frontier models are no longer “released and then spread everywhere.” Companies have to prove they have access controls, monitoring mechanisms, and safety commitments before they can win broader commercialization. Going forward, model competition may not only be about parameters, reasoning, and price, but also about who is better at connecting with regulatory systems.

B: The second thread is the market. AI-related tech stocks fell this week, and popular trades began amplifying volatility in reverse. Bloomberg mentioned that roughly $270 billion worth of modern speculative mechanisms were exposed by the market reversal, including leverage, ETFs, and momentum funds.

A: That does not mean the AI narrative is over. It means the valuation anchor has changed. Capital will not keep adding exposure just because of “AI growth.” Add to that Kashkari’s comment that there may still be one more rate hike this year, and the discount-rate pressure on high-valuation growth assets becomes more obvious. Crypto ETFs saw $4.5 billion in outflows in one week, which also shows that when risk appetite weakens, ETFs are not necessarily stabilizers. They may actually make exits smoother.

B: The third thread is energy. Demand for AI compute is pushing capital toward power assets. KKR agreed to acquire EDF’s renewable power business in the U.S. and Canada, and utility companies are also warning that the U.S. could face blackout risks because of power supply shortages.

A: This shows the bottleneck in the compute chain has moved further downstream from chips. Whether data centers can expand depends not only on GPUs, but also on long-term power purchasing capacity, grid access, and supply stability. Investors are now chasing energy infrastructure companies, but some of the technologies have not been fully proven, and valuations may already be pricing in future delivery ahead of time.

B: On the macro side, inflation risk has not left the stage either. The IMF’s chief economist said AI can push inflation higher not only through chip costs, but also through rising asset prices and a stronger wealth effect, which could bring stronger consumer demand. The European Central Bank’s Schnabel also warned that even if geopolitical tensions ease, inflation still carries upside risks.

A: So central banks are now watching not only energy and supply chains, but also whether the AI wealth effect will push up services consumption. On the other side, price hikes for iPads, Macs, and Xboxes also show that tight supply and demand for memory chips has already been passed through to consumer electronics. The more important on-device AI becomes, the harder it is to keep entry-level hardware configurations low-cost.

B: Geopolitics and trade were also very busy today. The U.S. launched a new round of strikes against Iran, which may weaken the already fragile ceasefire; but Israel, Lebanon, and the U.S. also reached a preliminary framework agreement, creating a partial cooling-off window for the Middle East.

A: On trade, Trump threatened 100% additional tariffs on countries that impose digital services taxes, pushing the taxation of tech platforms directly into goods trade negotiations. The USMCA is also about to enter its review stage, and expectations around rules for North American autos, electronics, and cross-border e-commerce may all be adjusted again.

B: Finally, a few structural shifts. Polymarket is being broadly investigated by the CFTC, and event contracts in prediction markets may face stricter compliance boundaries. After a U.S. insurance regulator suffered a cyberattack and suspended investment risk ratings, it also reminds us that the security risks of financial infrastructure itself are becoming a market variable.

A: On the developer side, Fission-AI/OpenSpec made it onto GitHub’s trending list, showing that AI programming is moving from “writing prompts” toward “writing specifications.” In the future, when teams use AI to write code, the key may not be who completes code faster, but who can manage requirements, acceptance criteria, and generation workflows more clearly.

B: To sum up, today’s keyword is not a single AI boom, but repricing. Frontier models are being repriced by regulation, tech assets are being repriced by interest rates and capital structures, compute is being repriced by power constraints, and global trade and geopolitical risks are also putting new price tags on corporate costs.
