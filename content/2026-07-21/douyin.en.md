U.S. stocks are hitting record highs, yet short positions have also surged to an all-time peak. Today’s biggest signal: the AI boom isn’t over—but the market has shifted from “believing the story” to “demanding profits.”

First, money is still chasing computing power. AI chip demand pushed South Korean exports to a record high for this period, while Nvidia’s Rubin has entered customer validation. That means capital spending is still translating into real orders.

Second, software companies are coming under pressure. Adobe and Salesforce are facing more sell ratings as investors worry that AI will erode traditional software’s per-seat revenue and pricing power. Going forward, simply saying you’ve “integrated AI” won’t be enough. Companies must prove it can generate new revenue.

Third, the boundaries of competition are changing. The U.S. is preparing to scrutinize the intellectual property sources behind Chinese models, while OpenAI’s next-generation model will be previewed early for government officials. Model competition has expanded beyond performance and price to compliance, security, and government approval.

So when judging the next phase of the AI rally, don’t just watch whether the indexes rise. What really matters is whether chip orders can hold up, software profits can materialize, and regulation will slow product rollouts. AI remains the main theme—but divergence and volatility will both intensify.
