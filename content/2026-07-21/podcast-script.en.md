Evaluation systems often have access to unreleased models, test data, and internal infrastructure at the same time, which can make them an attack vector in their own right. Going forward, assessing an AI company’s security will mean looking not only at whether its models have guardrails, but also at whether its entire evaluation pipeline can withstand real-world attacks.

Host A: Meanwhile, the United States is bringing frontier models more formally under government review. OpenAI CEO Sam Altman plans to brief government officials and members of Congress on its next-generation model, while the U.S. government is also tightening oversight of how widely some highly capable models can be released.

Host B: This will change the pace of product launches. In the past, companies mainly had to consider whether development was complete. Now, they may also face capability evaluations, confidentiality arrangements, and remediation requirements. Reviews can reduce the risk of misuse, but if the standards are not transparent, they could also delay launches and even give an added advantage to the small number of institutions authorized to access the models.

Host A: Regulatory pressure is also extending into international competition. U.S. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent said the government will examine whether Chinese open-source models show signs of intellectual property theft, while the White House is also discussing how to respond to low-cost Chinese models.

Host B: This means the basis of competition is expanding beyond performance and price to include the origins of training data and intellectual property. If a formal review mechanism takes shape, Chinese models entering the U.S. market may face longer reviews, more extensive evidence requirements, and potential restrictions on distribution channels. Whether their cost advantage can translate into global market share will increasingly depend on their compliance capabilities.

Host A: Now to the industry side. Z.AI plans to use only Chinese-made AI chips in a new large-scale data center, while Nvidia says the design of its next-generation Rubin platform has entered the customer delivery and validation stage.

Host B: These two stories are especially interesting when viewed together. For domestic Chinese computing infrastructure, the question is no longer whether chips can be manufactured, but whether large-scale clusters can operate reliably, whether the software is easy to use, and whether inference costs are competitive. Nvidia’s challenge is whether it can deliver its systems on schedule and enable customers to migrate smoothly. Chip competition is moving off the spec sheet and into production environments.

Host A: The integration of models and hardware may go even further. Google is reportedly developing a chip called Frozen v2 that hardwires parts of Gemini’s architecture directly into silicon, with the goal of significantly improving token efficiency per watt.

Host B: Specialized chips can reduce inference costs, but they also sacrifice flexibility. Designing and deploying a chip takes years. If the model architecture changes along the way, the efficiency advantage could turn into a sunk cost. What this project is really betting on is that Gemini’s core architecture will remain stable for long enough.

Host A: Capital markets, meanwhile, are showing a clear divide in their attitude toward AI. On one side, South Korean semiconductor exports have reached a record high for this period, and the Nasdaq has strengthened on a rebound in chip stocks. On the other, short positions in U.S. equities have risen to an all-time high, while Adobe and Salesforce are receiving more sell ratings.

Host B: Real-world demand has not disappeared, but investors are starting to distinguish between companies that genuinely benefit and those that do not. Chips and memory have already translated capital spending into export growth, while traditional software faces the risk that AI will weaken seat counts, pricing power, and renewal rates. The fact that indexes are rising while short positions are also increasing shows that capital is still chasing AI, but is becoming more cautious about high valuations and whether earnings will actually materialize.

Host A: Let’s turn to broader macroeconomic risks. Escalating conflict between the United States and Iran has already pushed up the cost of liquefied natural gas purchases in Asia, while the United States has also threatened to impose 50 percent tariffs on some Canadian goods.

Host B: Both developments are turning political risk into real costs for businesses and public finances. Pakistan and Bangladesh must pay higher prices for natural gas, while North American automotive and agricultural supply chains may need to reassess inventory, pricing, and sourcing arrangements. The AI trade has temporarily overshadowed these pressures, but energy costs and tariffs will ultimately still feed into inflation, interest rates, and corporate profits.

The open-source project llmfit can automatically detect which models are suitable for local hardware, reducing the manual trial and error involved in estimating VRAM requirements, choosing quantization options, and comparing providers.

The key question going forward will no longer be whether a model can run, but whether its real-world throughput, output quality, and hardware utilization justify the investment.
