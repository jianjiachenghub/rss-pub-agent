Title: Today’s Must-Read Stories Go Beyond AI

July 21 was packed with developments. AI remained the main theme, but risks across capital markets, cybersecurity, trade, and energy were also rising. Here are the six key takeaways:

1️⃣ AI competition enters the “workflow era”

OpenAI’s AI agents have surpassed 10 million users. The industry is no longer competing solely on model benchmarks, but on who can actually complete tasks for users, connect with more tools, and become the gateway to everyday work.

Alibaba is also frequently testing Qwen3.8-Max-Preview. The key things to watch next are pricing, stability, tool use, and whether the official release will be open-source.

2️⃣ AI faces tighter government scrutiny

Sam Altman will brief U.S. officials on OpenAI’s next-generation model, while the U.S. is also preparing to examine the intellectual property origins of Chinese open-source models.

This suggests that frontier model launches may soon face a new stage of government pre-review. U.S.-China AI competition is also expanding beyond performance and pricing to training data, intellectual property, and market access.

3️⃣ The computing-power race splits into two paths

On one side, Nvidia’s Rubin has entered customer validation, further strengthening its ecosystem across chips, systems, and software.

On the other, Z.AI plans to use domestically produced AI chips throughout its large data centers. The next test for China’s chip substitution drive is not just shipment volume, but cluster stability, software compatibility, and inference cost per unit.

Google is even reportedly planning to hardwire parts of Gemini’s architecture directly into chips for a six- to tenfold improvement in energy efficiency—delivering greater efficiency at the cost of flexibility in model iteration.

4️⃣ The market is increasingly divided on AI

South Korean exports reached a record high for the period, showing that AI-driven semiconductor demand is already flowing into physical trade. Low-cost models may not necessarily hurt chipmakers either: lower application costs could instead stimulate greater demand for computing power and storage.

On the other hand, short positions in U.S. stocks have reached an all-time high, while Adobe and Salesforce have both been downgraded. The market is starting to ask whether new AI revenue can offset the erosion of traditional software seats and pricing power.

This is no longer simply about being bullish or bearish on AI. It is about reassessing who can actually make money from it.

5️⃣ Don’t overlook security risks

OpenAI and Hugging Face have disclosed that model evaluation environments are becoming entry points for sophisticated cyberattacks. The widely used tool 7-Zip has also revealed a buffer-overflow vulnerability that could enable remote code execution.

For businesses and developers, models, plugins, evaluation sandboxes, and local toolchains must now all be treated as part of supply-chain security.

6️⃣ Beyond AI, three types of risk are heating up

The U.S. has threatened 50% tariffs on certain Canadian goods, putting pressure on North American automotive and agricultural supply chains. U.S.-Iran tensions are driving up Asia’s liquefied natural gas procurement costs. Russia’s suspension of bond auctions has also exposed pressure from interest rates and fiscal financing.

Meanwhile, Singapore is leading Southeast Asia in attracting record foreign investment, showing that supply-chain relocation continues to reshape Asia’s capital landscape.

📌 Today’s core takeaway:

The AI rally is not over, but the industry has moved beyond the “bigger models are always better” phase. Products must now prove themselves simultaneously through real-world adoption, regulatory scrutiny, computing efficiency, and actual profitability.

The four variables most worth watching next are AI agent retention rates, the real-world performance of domestic chips, U.S. model-review rules, and how geopolitical conflicts feed through to energy costs and valuations.
