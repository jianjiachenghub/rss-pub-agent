For markets, what's scariest isn't rate hikes or cuts—it's not being able to predict where the road leads next. Volatility definitely won't be small.

**[Section Two: New Winds in the Tech World]**

**Host A:** After macro, let's come back to the tech world. Although the broader environment is uncertain, developers haven't stopped optimizing. The Java tool library AutoScan released version 1.3.0 today, with a practical update—added regex package filtering.

**Host B:** This sounds like a detail, but it's very friendly for Spring Boot developers. Before, component scanning was often a blanket approach, loading a lot of unnecessary beans and slowing down startup. Now with regex filtering and environment conditional configuration, the granularity has gone from "package" to "class pattern," directly optimizing the application's startup cost structure.

**Host A:** This kind of extreme pursuit of performance is the same on mobile. HarmonyOS has a new solution using system Picker plus TaskPool and ImagePacker to smooth out the image processing pipeline—they say "don't let image compression drag down first frame."

**Host B:** Actually, the core idea is "separation of concerns." Stripping heavy computation tasks from the UI thread to a background TaskPool—this should be the standard paradigm for mobile image-intensive applications. The user experience improvement is immediate.

**[Section Three: AI's Efficiency and Reality]**

**Host A:** Next, let's talk about AI, which everyone's most concerned about. Today there's an interesting contrast. On one side, Musk says Grok is approaching Claude Opus 4.6 levels, but we'll have to wait until May or even June. In the AI world, a month or two is actually a long time.

**Host B:** It shows how intense the competition is—everyone's racing to push model capabilities. But the news on the other side is more thought-provoking: an online medical platform called Medvi, with just two people plus AI tools, achieved $400 million in revenue, with projections of 1.8 billion next year.

**Host A:** This sounds like an inspirational story—two people building a billion-scale business. But looking closer at the analysis, this is actually a classic case of AI reshaping cost structures. Traditional platforms might need dozens or hundreds of people; now it's been compressed to the extreme.

**Host B:** But let's also add a reality check. The article mentioned that its revenue mainly comes from high-demand categories like GLP-1 weight-loss drugs. How much did AI actually contribute to supply chain and compliance, or did they just ride the category's wave? That's still up for debate. This leads to today's most striking article—"The 'Electricity Paradox' of the AI Era."

**Host A:** This perspective is particularly sharp. The article says individual efficiency might increase 10 times, but company value might yield nothing. It's like 19th century textile mills switching to electric motors—output didn't change until they redesigned the assembly line.

**Host B:** Exactly, just replacing the engine without redesigning the factory means the dividend can't be realized. Many companies now just treat AI as a tool to speed up individuals, but forget to simultaneously restructure their organization and processes. This might be the biggest challenge for companies in the coming years.
