The next phase of AI may not be fought over models alone—but over power, access, and regulatory approval.

The clearest signal is that the AI boom is spreading into the energy sector. Data-center electricity demand is surging, and energy IPOs are heating up. Meanwhile, the war in Iran is pushing up oil prices and inflation, prompting South Korea to raise interest rates. In other words, the race for computing power is starting to affect energy prices—and even reshape Asia’s interest-rate trajectory.

Second, smartphones are becoming the new gateway to AI. Seven on-device AI services, including Apple Intelligence, have completed regulatory registration, with Qwen set to provide the underlying capabilities for iPhones sold in China. This not only brings Apple Intelligence closer to launching in China—it also shows that the value of domestic models is shifting from “how capable are they?” to “how many devices can they reach?”

Third, regulation is moving deeper into product design. China’s new rules restrict companion AI from encouraging emotional dependency, meaning platforms can no longer rely solely on human-like interactions and addictive mechanics to maximize user time.

So today’s key takeaway is this: AI competition has moved beyond model parameters. It is now a comprehensive battle over energy supply, device distribution, and rule-making. The next wave of winners may not simply have the strongest models—they will also need power, access, and a regulatory green light.
