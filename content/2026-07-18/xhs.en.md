Today’s news is packed: AI permissions, Middle East energy risks, and chip-sector deleveraging are all heating up

If you only read a few stories, these are the 5 most important things to watch today👇

1️⃣ The more powerful AI becomes, the more cautiously its permissions must be managed

OpenAI has acknowledged that GPT-5.6-powered Codex may accidentally delete files from users’ home directories when sandboxing is disabled, full access is enabled, and automatic approvals are turned on.

What really matters here is not a single incident, but the need to rethink permission models for coding agents: greater capability should not mean greater access. For companies adopting AI coding tools, sandbox isolation, human confirmation, and file recovery mechanisms will likely become non-negotiable requirements.

Meanwhile, Google’s Gemini 3.5 Pro has reportedly been delayed after falling short of coding-performance targets, while the open-source project Wigolo is exploring ways to give agents internet access at zero API cost. Industry competition is shifting from “How smart is the model?” to “Can it complete tasks safely, reliably, and affordably?”

2️⃣ Middle East risks are beginning to disrupt actual shipping

Two oil tankers reportedly exploded and caught fire south of the Strait of Hormuz, while Iran’s Revolutionary Guard declared the strait extremely dangerous and closed. At the same time, strikes between the US and Iran are escalating, with signs that the conflict may be spilling over into Saudi Arabia.

The market’s greatest concern is that the risk is no longer limited to verbal threats and could genuinely disrupt energy shipments. The key questions now are how long the strait remains closed, how long inventories can last, and whether alternative routes have enough capacity. Oil, natural gas, fertilizer, shipping, and insurance costs may all be repriced.

3️⃣ South Korea’s chip rally is entering a deleveraging phase

The KOSPI has fallen roughly 25% from its peak in less than a month. Samsung and SK Hynix have lost a combined 940 trillion won in market value, 1.2 million accounts have received margin calls, and many positions have already been forcibly liquidated.

This means the market is no longer focused solely on chip-sector earnings, but also on whether investors can withstand their leverage. Once forced selling gains momentum, it could amplify the decline further.

4️⃣ Kimi is challenging both the technology rankings and capital markets

Kimi K3 topped a web-engineering benchmark with a 92% task success rate, showing that open-source models are beginning to challenge proprietary coding products. Reports also suggest that Moonshot AI could list in Hong Kong within six months.

What matters is not “how many parameters it has,” but whether its task success rate, execution speed, and operating costs can be replicated consistently. If they can, proprietary coding models will face pressure on both pricing and distribution advantages.

5️⃣ Regulation and geopolitics present two more variables

The SEC plans to make quarterly financial reporting voluntary rather than mandatory. If implemented, investors may receive corporate information less frequently, increasing the risk of information asymmetry.

In Ukraine, President Zelensky is reportedly considering dismissing the commander-in-chief as protests expand, adding further uncertainty to military and political stability.

Today in one sentence:

The technology sector is rethinking the balance between “capability and permission,” financial markets are digesting “high valuations and high leverage,” and the situation in the Middle East is shifting from a sentiment-driven risk toward a tangible threat to physical supplies.
