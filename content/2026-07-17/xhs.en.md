Title: Today’s Must-Reads Go Beyond AI

July 17 brought a flood of news, but the stories that truly matter can be distilled into five key themes:

1️⃣ AI Is Learning to “Read Less Code”

The trending GitHub project code-review-graph builds a code dependency graph in advance, allowing coding agents to read only the parts that are genuinely relevant.

For large projects, this is more practical than simply expanding the context window: it reduces full-repository scans, saves tokens, and makes code reviews more consistent.

2️⃣ Chinese AI Models Enter a New Round of Competition

Kimi K3 pushes MoE, million-token context windows, and native multimodality even further. Meanwhile, Zhipu is reportedly generating $1 billion in ARR—a 15-fold increase in six months.

One is competing on model capabilities; the other is testing the speed of commercialization. However, Zhipu has not officially confirmed these figures, and questions remain about how revenue is defined, renewal rates, and customer concentration.

Cybersecurity has also become a new testing ground. Reports suggest that Chinese models are narrowing the gap with U.S. models on related tasks.

3️⃣ AI Shifts from “Able to Operate” to “Authorized to Act”

The new generation of Doubao phones no longer reads screens or simulates taps. Instead, it only connects to apps willing to provide MCP access, data, and operational permissions.

This means what an AI phone can do no longer depends solely on model capabilities, but also on whether major platforms such as WeChat and Taobao are willing to open their interfaces. The next battleground may be commercial negotiations.

Meanwhile, 1Password is beginning to help agents such as Claude access login credentials. The experience is becoming smoother, but excessive permissions, unintended actions, and credential exposure will create new security risks.

4️⃣ AI Is Booming, but Capital Markets Are Cooling

AI-related stocks reversed course, putting global tech assets under pressure. South Korea’s stock market has fallen 25% from its peak, triggering forced liquidations across many retail accounts, while regulators have begun restricting leveraged ETFs tied to individual stocks.

Elsewhere, Databricks’ potential valuation has risen to $188 billion, while Japan continues buying Nvidia chips. Capital is still betting on AI, but the tension between lofty valuations, high leverage, and real returns is becoming increasingly apparent.

That makes OpenAI’s proposed “AI scorecard” especially timely: don’t just look at usage volume—measure how much useful work gets done, the cost per successful task, reliability, and the return on compute investment.

5️⃣ Geopolitics Is Affecting Everyone’s Wallet

China is promoting open-source AI across developing countries, while Japan is investing more heavily in sovereign computing capacity. AI has entered the competition for national technological influence.

Meanwhile, conflict in the Middle East is pushing UK mortgage rates higher again. The controversy over nationalizing British Steel is also undermining confidence in cross-border investment. Technology, finance, and geopolitical risk are becoming ever more tightly intertwined.

The one sentence worth remembering today:

The focus of AI competition is shifting from “how large is the model?” to “can it secure data and permissions, generate real revenue, and operate reliably amid security risks and market volatility?”
