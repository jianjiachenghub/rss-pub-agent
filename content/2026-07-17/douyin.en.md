The real dividing line in AI is no longer who has more parameters, but who can turn capability into revenue, access, and reliable output.

The clearest signal: Zhipu AI reportedly grew its ARR fifteenfold in six months, reaching $1 billion. The figure has not been confirmed by the company, but it suggests that high-frequency use cases like coding are emerging as the most promising cash-flow engines for large models.

Meanwhile, Kimi K3 pushes open-source models to 2.8 trillion parameters and a one-million-token context window. Code-review-graph takes another critical path: building a code dependency graph first, so agents read only the files that truly matter. One raises the capability ceiling; the other lowers the cost of use.

The gateways to AI are changing too. The new Doubao phone no longer simulates taps inside apps—it gains access through MCP interfaces. That means what AI phones can do will increasingly depend on whether platforms are willing to open up.

But capital markets are already demanding returns: AI stocks have reversed course, while highly leveraged tech trades in Asia are facing forced liquidations.

So today’s key takeaway is this: AI competition is moving from “showcasing capability” to “proving commercial efficiency.” From here on, don’t just watch parameter counts. Watch revenue quality, cost per task, and whether platforms are willing to open their gates.
