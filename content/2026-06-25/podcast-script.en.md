**"Personal Daily" Podcast Script · Today's Highlights**

---

**[Opening]**

**A:** Hey, today we need to talk about a clear shift happening in the AI industry.  
**B:** Right. Before, everyone was competing over model parameters and how smart the chatbots were, but looking at today's headlines, it feels like the battlefield has already shifted.  
**A:** Exactly. Whether it's US tech giants scrambling for electricity, chip manufacturers beating earnings expectations, or Doubao starting to charge subscription fees—fundamentally, they're all pointing to one thing: AI is moving from the "storytelling" phase into one where it's all about hard capabilities and commercial implementation. This is probably the most important industry narrative worth watching today.

---

**[Theme 1: Electricity is Compute]**

**B:** Let's start with something interesting. Amazon and Google are now going all-out scrambling for electricity.  
**A:** Right. AI companies fighting over power resources used to sound like a fringe topic, but now it's become a core strategy. Think about it—large model training and inference, it's all backed by data centers, and data centers are power hogs.  
**B:** Amazon has its existing cloud infrastructure advantage. What about Google?  
**A:** Google seems to be making innovations in clean energy and data center efficiency. Bottom line, whoever has stable, cheap, green electricity in the future can run a few more rounds in the model arms race. The compute competition is ultimately an energy competition.

---

**[Theme 2: Real Demand at the Chip Level]**

**B:** Speaking of hardware, Micron reported earnings last night. They easily beat expectations, with revenue and profit outlooks higher than Wall Street predicted.  
**A:** This is a pretty crucial signal. Before, there were plenty of AI skeptics in the market thinking NVIDIA had run up too much, wondering if AI demand was overhyped. With Micron's results out—storage chips selling like crazy—it shows downstream demand is genuinely solid.  
**B:** Right. AI doesn't just need GPUs; HBM high-bandwidth memory is also core.  
**A:** Plus Micron surged after hours and even boosted Nasdaq futures. This shows capital voting with its feet: the hardware cycle driven by AI might last longer than many people imagined.

---

**[Theme 3: Domestic Apps Testing Commercialization]**

**B:** Looking at domestic news now. Doubao, under ByteDance, officially launched three tiers of paid subscriptions, ranging from 68 yuan to 500 yuan per month.  
**A:** My first reaction seeing this was—domestic large models are also starting to seriously figure out how to make money.  
**B:** The pricing tiers are pretty interesting. The highest tier offers 10 times the quota of the standard version, plus it can handle Agent tasks, operate local computers, and execute on schedules—sounds like providing an enterprise or individual studio with a "digital employee."  
**A:** And they deliberately kept a free version, promising continuous model updates for free users. This shows ByteDance also understands that at this stage, they can't afford to lose either the user base or the paid conversion.  
**B:** But at the end of the day, if an Agent task errors out halfway through execution, that's a killer for user experience. 500 yuan isn't expensive for enterprises, but for individual users... whether they renew will probably depend on how stable it actually is.

---

**[Closing]**

**A:** So connecting these three news items today, it's quite interesting. The US side is going hard on "heavy assets" like electricity and chip manufacturing, while domestically they're urgently searching for monetization paths at the application layer.  
**B:** Different paths, but the same goal: turning AI from a lab technology into a sustainable, properly functioning industry. Alright, that's it for today.  
**A:** See you next time.
