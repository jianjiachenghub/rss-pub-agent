**A:** Yeah, looking at it now, they’ll likely move in June or July. This means Europe might enter the easing cycle before the Fed does. This kind of policy divergence will have real impacts on exchange rates and cross-border capital flows.

**B:** Okay, back to AI. Nvidia keeps hitting new highs recently, but the controversy is growing too—right now Wall Street is arguing over one thing: is this an industrial revolution or an asset bubble?

**A:** It’s a bit of both. On one hand, the demand is real—OpenAI and Anthropic’s growth is genuinely pulling the entire supply chain along. But on the other hand, this pace of growth has definitely priced in some expectations early. Speaking of real demand, the comparison between these domestic large model companies is pretty interesting.

**B:** That 36Kr piece?

**A:** Yeah, Zhipu, MiniMax, Moonshot, and DeepSeek—these "four cash-burning giants"—are in completely different situations. MiniMax is reportedly already profitable, thanks to overseas revenue. DeepSeek is still in the basic research phase, relying on the parent company for funding. Zhipu has large revenue but also large losses. This shows investors can’t just generically bet on the AI concept anymore. You have to distinguish whether you’re tech-driven, product-driven, or cash-burn-driven. Profitability has become a hard metric.

**B:** There’s another dimension that’s more serious. The *Financial Times* says the Iranian military is using ChatGPT. Although they didn’t reveal exactly what for, there’s huge room for speculation—intelligence analysis, cognitive warfare, cyber operations, all are possible.

**A:** This really changes how we assess the proliferation risks of large models. Previously, we thought of state-level cyberattack capabilities as the threshold, but now the barrier has lowered—a language model can become a tool for geopolitical confrontation. This poses new challenges for export controls and security reviews on AI companies.

**B:** The capital side is also reshuffling. SoftBank plans to pour 75 billion euros into France’s AI hub, trying to make France the European AI center. Meanwhile, the successful exits of SpaceX and OpenAI have global capital eyeing Asia for the next wave of opportunities.

**A:** But this enthusiasm isn’t without headwinds. Denmark’s AkademikerPension, which manages $25 billion in assets, recently blacklisted SpaceX.

**B:** ESG reasons?

**A:** Yeah, they think there are corporate governance issues, and the valuation is severely inflated. This shows that even for star unicorns, ESG and valuation reasonableness are increasingly becoming hard constraints—you can’t just raise money on narratives anymore.

**B:** Finally, a recovery signal from the industry chain. STMicroelectronics raised prices for the second time this year—both MCUs and power semiconductors are going up. This is direct evidence that industry sentiment is recovering, showing that the previous phase of order cuts and inventory destocking is over, and supply-demand dynamics are improving.

**A:** Right. Today we mapped out several threads—from monetary policy to AI militarization, from capital divergence to the semiconductor cycle. AI has definitely entered deep waters. It’s no longer just toys for tech geeks, but a multi-dimensional game involving macro trends, geopolitics, and corporate governance.

**B:** Yeah, investment logic is changing along with it. Alright, that’s it for today.
