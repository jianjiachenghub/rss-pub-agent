The most important takeaway today is this: the AI trade is moving from “storytelling” into “doing the math.”

In the past, as long as the market heard about AI spending, compute expansion, or model upgrades, it was willing to pay high valuations. But now it’s different. Capital is starting to ask three questions: Who is actually making profits? Who is carrying the capital expenditure? And who gets repriced first when liquidity tightens?

The first signal is Microsoft. Even with cloud, Copilot, and enterprise access, its stock has still come under heavy pressure this month, wiping out hundreds of billions of dollars in market value. That shows the market is no longer rewarding AI investment alone. It wants to see whether that investment can turn into revenue and profit.

The second signal comes from chips and power. Samsung and SK are still increasing semiconductor capacity, while AI is also driving up M&A activity in the U.S. power sector. In other words, the real near-term winners from AI may not be application companies, but the infrastructure players: chips, memory, and electricity.

The third signal is regulation and financial risk. The Bank for International Settlements has already warned that AI mania could threaten the global economy, and the market is starting to worry about deleveraging events in highly valued assets.

So the core point today is not that AI is fading. It’s that AI is starting to split into layers: those selling the shovels keep taking the meat, while those raising money on stories are starting to face hard questions. In the next phase, don’t just watch who talks about AI. Watch who can turn AI into cash flow.
