The most important takeaway today is this: competition in AI is shifting from “whose model is smarter” to “who can more reliably deliver compute, state, and real production capacity.”

First, the hardware side. Foxconn’s quarterly sales jumped 40% year over year, which shows that Nvidia demand is not just sitting in chip orders. It has already flowed through to server assembly and full-system delivery. At the same time, Micron is expanding HBM capacity in Japan, and SK Hynix is reportedly planning a U.S. listing to compete for AI memory investors.

But the problem is short-term supply cannot keep up. Prices for 64GB DIMMs rose 3.5 times from the third quarter of last year to the first quarter of this year, and could climb to 5 times. In other words, AI cost accounting is no longer just about GPUs. Memory is becoming a key variable too.

Now look at the software side. Projects like planning-with-files and gastown gaining traction on GitHub show that AI coding agents are catching up on the basics: long-running tasks need to resume, multiple agents need to collaborate, and state needs to be auditable.

Finally, Doubao taking its agent feature offline is also a meaningful signal. General-purpose character agents are starting to fade, and platforms will put resources into higher-frequency, more predictable use cases.

In one sentence: in the second half of AI, the contest is not about demo effects. It is about supply capacity, engineering foundations, and business certainty.
