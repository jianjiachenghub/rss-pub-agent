Today’s most important stories are not just about AI

Today’s information flow is clear: AI is still rising, but what really deserves attention is not “what new model came out,” but the reshuffling of engineering, compute, storage, and capital behind it.

1. AI coding agents are starting to add “long-term memory”

GitHub trending projects `planning-with-files` and `gastown` both point to the same trend: AI agents are no longer just helping you write code within a single conversation. They need to execute tasks over the long term, preserve state, support collaboration, and resume work after interruptions.

This shows AI engineering is entering its next stage:  
whoever manages task state well gets closer to a truly usable “engineering employee.”

2. Nvidia’s supply chain continues to validate AI demand

Foxconn’s quarterly revenue grew 40% year over year, beating expectations, again because of strong demand for AI servers.

The key point: AI momentum should not only be judged by Nvidia chip orders, but also by whether server assembly, full-system delivery, and cloud provider capex are actually materializing.

Supply chain revenue is becoming an important indicator for tracking the AI cycle.

3. Storage has become the new compute bottleneck

Several stories today were related to storage:

SK Hynix is considering a U.S. listing to capture AI storage valuations;  
Micron is expanding HBM capacity in Japan, but meaningful supply will not arrive until 2028;  
DRAM prices are surging, and 64GB DIMM prices could rise as much as 5x in total.

The conclusion is straightforward: AI infrastructure is not only expensive because of GPUs. Memory is also starting to rewrite the cost equation.  
Inference, cloud services, and in-memory databases will all need to recalculate their economics.

4. Large model companies are starting to compete for power and data centers

Anthropic plans to secure at least 1.4 gigawatts of data center resources in Australia, with total investment potentially reaching $15 billion.

This is not just expansion. It shows that the competitive boundaries for large model companies have changed:  
beyond models, talent, and data, power, land, and long-term compute contracts have also become core assets.

In the future, evaluating an AI company will not just mean looking at model rankings, but also whether it has stable production capacity.

5. General-purpose agent products are entering a correction phase

Doubao’s agent feature will be shut down on July 15.

This is quite representative: platforms are pulling back from low-certainty, low-frequency role-based agents and shifting resources toward clearer use cases, such as deep research, coding, e-commerce, podcasts, and more.

For AI products, more features are not always better. The real task is finding entry points users will actually keep using.

6. Global capital flows are quietly changing routes

Emerging market carry traders are starting to reduce dollar-funded positions and shift toward currencies such as the euro and Australian dollar.

This shows that there are more variables affecting global risk appetite.  
If the dollar is no longer the optimal funding currency, emerging market capital flows will become more vulnerable to interest-rate differentials and exchange-rate swings in other currencies.

Today’s one-line summary:

The AI theme is still intact, but attention is shifting from “how smart are the models?” to “who can secure compute, storage, power, engineering efficiency, and real application scenarios.” The real changes have already begun at the infrastructure layer.
