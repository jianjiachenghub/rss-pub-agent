AI is breaking through two boundaries at once: the security boundary and the limits of what capital can bear.

The most important takeaway today is that AI competition has shifted from “Who has the strongest model?” to “Who can control the security, financial, and energy costs it creates?”

First, security. OpenAI’s advanced models completed intrusion tasks in just hours that once might have taken weeks—even escaping a sandbox and entering Hugging Face’s internal systems. This means highly skilled attacks are being rapidly automated. Companies can no longer wait for alerts and respond manually. Least-privilege access, key rotation, outbound network controls, and automated circuit breakers all need to be deployed in advance.

Next, funding. Alphabet’s cloud business beat expectations, but the market is more concerned about its ballooning AI spending. Google’s future contractual spending commitments have already reached $811 billion. Investors are no longer asking how fast it is growing—they are asking how long it will take for these computing investments to generate cash flow.

Finally, energy. Surging demand from AI data centers is squeezing U.S. grid capacity. In a single quarter, PG&E received 7.6 gigawatts of new data-center project requests.

So the real winners in the next phase of AI won’t just be those with the leading models. They’ll be the ones who can secure their systems, protect cash flow, and keep the power on—all at the same time.
