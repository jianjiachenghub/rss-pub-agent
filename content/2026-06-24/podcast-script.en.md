**Podcast Script: "Personal Daily" Morning Edition**

**Hosts:** A (Ge), B (Bei)

**Duration:** Approx. 5 minutes

---

**[Opening]**

**A:** Hey, have you noticed there's a really clear through-line in today's tech news? The AI competition has moved from algorithm models to the infrastructure level—or rather, from "soft" to "hard."

**B:** Right. Chips, power, data centers—these physical constraints are becoming the real bottlenecks. Whether industry giants or national policies, everyone's repositioning for these "hard constraints." Today, from this angle, let's pick out a few things worth talking about.

---

**[Item One: Custom Chips and the Compute Arms Race]**

**A:** First, the hardware. OpenAI and Broadcom have partnered to launch a custom chip called "Jalapeño," specifically optimized for inference tasks in large language models.

**B:** This is a practical move. Right now, AI companies' cost pressure is mainly on inference, not training. Using custom chips to reduce long-term operating costs shows the industry logic is shifting from "burning cash to tell stories" to "counting pennies to build moats."

**A:** Exactly. And this confirms another big move we're seeing—SK Hynix plans to raise nearly $30 billion in a US IPO, a record-breaking amount. As the leader in HBM high-bandwidth memory, they're clearly riding the wave of AI storage demand to dig deeper moats around their production capacity.

---

**[Item Two: Energy Bottlenecks and Physical Constraints]**

**B:** That said, chips are only half the problem. The other half is electricity. Look at Sunrun—this residential solar company signed an agreement with Tesla to supply power for AI projects, and their stock jumped 30% straight away.

**A:** But paradoxically, communities and local governments across the US are resisting new data centers—noise, power consumption, environmental impact. This creates a real tension: AI needs more electricity, but residents don't want data centers in their backyards.

**B:** So nuclear solutions are back on the agenda. Canada's AtkinsRealis is seeking US approval to use nuclear technology to power AI data centers. Looks like solving AI's power bottleneck might really depend on breakthroughs in basic energy infrastructure.

---

**[Item Three: Geopolitics and Supply Chains]**

**A:** Beyond hardware and energy, there's another layer of "hard constraints": geopolitics. Multiple EU countries have joined a US-led agreement to reduce dependence on China's AI supply chain.

**B:** Meanwhile, transatlantic political winds are shifting. Trump is losing European allies—even right-wing nationalist politicians like Italy's Meloni and France's Le Pen are keeping their distance from him. This could change how Europe and the US interact on tech cooperation going forward.

**A:** Also, new developments in the Russia-Ukraine situation—Russia is pressuring Belarus, potentially opening a second front. If that happens, geopolitical risks in Eastern Europe will escalate significantly, which is a hidden danger for global supply chains and market sentiment.

---

**[Item Four: Market Divergence and Regulatory Evolution]**

**B:** Back to the capital markets—sentiment is clearly diverging. SoftBank's Masayoshi Son said calling it an "AI bubble" is an insult to practitioners, but Korean stock market volatility hit record highs due to AI concerns, and Indian IT stocks' weight in the index fell to historic lows.

**A:** Also, Cerebras stock plunged due to weak 2026 sales outlook. This shows investors are separating the real from the fake—not every company touching AI will benefit. So Micron's earnings tonight become especially critical; the market desperately needs to see real demand data to justify valuations.

**B:** Finally, a softer change. Major AI labs are hiring philosophers in large numbers to handle ethics and safety alignment issues, while the Anthropic incident exposed regulatory chaos. Technology and hardware are racing ahead, but the infrastructure of humanities and regulation also needs to catch up.

---

**[Closing]**

**A:** It looks like the AI industry is moving from adolescent frenzy to adult pragmatism—needing to build chips, secure power, navigate geopolitics, and handle complex regulation and ethics.

**B:** Right. Going forward, it's no longer about who has the bigger model, but rather who can balance these hard and soft constraints. That's today's curated content. See you tomorrow.
