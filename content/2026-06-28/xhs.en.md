Title: Today It’s Not Just AI. Markets Have Big News Too

I’ve organized today’s most important information by “why it matters”:

**1. AI compute is becoming a hard constraint**

Google restricting Meta’s use of Gemini is not really a small business relationship dispute. At its core, compute is running short. In the future, when big tech companies buy model services, they may look not only at price and capability, but also at supply priority.

DeepSeek and Peking University open-sourcing DSpark is also important: it improves high-concurrency inference speed by 60% to 85%. This shows that AI product experience is moving from “how strong is the model?” to “how fast, stable, and cost-efficient is it during peak usage?”

**2. AI investment fever is spilling over into power, financing, and data centers**

European capital is starting to look at power suppliers and banks, while Firmus is also planning to build a data center in Indonesia with Nvidia. The AI theme is no longer just about chips and models. Power, land, offtake contracts, and financing capacity are all being repriced.

But the other side also deserves attention: the Bank for International Settlements warns that AI investment mania could lead to a long-term investment slump, and the tech stock selloff has made markets worry again about AI-driven debt expansion. Put simply, AI is still hot, but capital is starting to ask: after the money is burned, where is the cash flow?

**3. The key risks in financial markets are “leverage” and “interest rates”**

Leveraged money behind the earlier rise in U.S. stocks is starting to make investors nervous. If gains are mainly driven by financing rather than realized earnings, volatility and margin pressure will become more sensitive later.

Emerging market bonds are also under pressure: lower energy prices should have been positive, but a hawkish Fed will keep increasing pressure from higher dollar interest rates.

In China, regulators are stepping up their crackdown on illegal offshore investment, affecting cross-border capital flows, financial institution reviews, and expectations for RMB stability.

**4. The financing window for Hong Kong stocks and AI is reopening**

Hong Kong’s equity financing in the first half of the year rose to a five-year high, with the AI boom helping revive stock issuance. The signal is direct: as long as the narrative is strong enough, the primary market window can still open, especially for AI-related assets.

**5. Developer tools are moving in several new directions**

video-use has gone viral, suggesting video editing may shift from manual timelines toward Agent + scripted workflows.

lingbot-map focuses on streaming 3D reconstruction, which matters for robotics, AR, and digital twins.

Local offline speech-to-text tools like FluidVoice are also worth watching, because privacy, latency, and API costs will become new selling points for personal productivity tools.

**6. Embodied intelligence continues to attract capital**

General Intuition raised $320 million, using game footage and player operation data to train action models. The key point here is not “AI playing games,” but that capital is starting to treat “visuals + action labels” as an embodied intelligence asset.

**7. Policy and energy are also sending several signals**

The U.S. Supreme Court has left Trump-related cases involving the Federal Reserve and birthright citizenship for last, and markets will watch the risk to Fed independence.

The largest wind farm in the U.S. has entered operation, but overall industry installation speed may slow, showing that the energy transition is not accelerating in a straight line.

Japan’s government draft favors “appropriate” monetary policy, which in practice is lowering expectations for further rate hikes. Both the yen and Japanese bonds will be affected.

One-sentence summary:  
Today’s main thread is not “AI is hot,” but that AI is pulling compute, power, debt, capital markets, and developer tools into the same cycle; at the same time, global interest rates and regulatory variables are reminding markets that behind the excitement, costs and cash flow still matter.
