The most important signal today is this: the AI boom is still spreading, but the market has started asking one question: are there really enough money and compute?

First, compute. Because AI demand is so tight, Google has limited the scale at which Meta can use Gemini. This shows that large-model competition is not just about model capability. It is about supply priority. Whoever has compute has the ability to deliver reliably.

Second, capital is also starting to reprice AI. The Bank for International Settlements warned that AI investment mania could lead to long-term stagnation, while a tech stock selloff has triggered new concerns over AI-driven debt expansion. In other words, if high valuations depend on continuous financing instead of cash flow actually materializing, volatility will be bigger down the road.

At the same time, AI infrastructure is still looking for new outlets. European capital is starting to rotate from tech stocks into power suppliers and banks, while Firmus plans to build data centers in Indonesia with Nvidia. The AI theme is expanding from “buy models, buy chips” into electricity, financing, land, and data centers.

On the domestic technology front, DeepSeek and Peking University have open-sourced DSpark, boosting high-concurrency inference speed by 60% to 85%. This kind of optimization matters because it directly affects what users feel: is it fast, is it stable, and is it expensive?

So today’s takeaway is clear: AI is not receding. It is entering a cost-review phase. What will truly matter next is not just companies that can tell a good story, but companies that can make compute, cash flow, and delivery efficiency actually work.
