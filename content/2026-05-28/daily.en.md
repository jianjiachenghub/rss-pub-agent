# Personal Daily Report | May 28, 2026

## Today's Take

> AI investment is experiencing a tale of two extremes: ByteDance's $70 billion infrastructure gamble contrasts sharply with Uber burning through its budget in four months, signaling an industry shift from irrational expansion to strict ROI scrutiny. Meanwhile, the AWS-Snowflake $6 billion chip deal reinforces cloud giants' moats, while the Gemini 3.5 repository deletion incident runs parallel to Claude Code's stability upgrades, revealing that engineering deployment has entered a "reliability-first" phase. Open-source Agent frameworks are emerging densely, with product forms penetrating vertical scenarios like payments and voice, yet the divergence between capital efficiency and technical maturity is widening.

## AI

### ByteDance Discusses Spending Up to $70 Billion This Year on AI Infrastructure

**Event:** ByteDance is discussing a spending plan of up to $70 billion (approx. RMB 475.9 billion) for this year, primarily for building data centers and other AI infrastructure. Much of the funding will come from its roughly $50 billion (approx. RMB 339.9 billion) profit in 2025. The current figures are preliminary and will be adjusted quarterly. This year's capital expenditure could reach RMB 400–500 billion. If economic and business conditions prove ideal, next year's capex may rise further to approximately $100 billion (approx. RMB 679.9 billion), compared to roughly $25 billion last year. This initiative will help the company solidify its leadership in AI chatbots, video generation, and other fields while expanding its overseas influence. Additionally, because data center construction costs are lower in China than in the US, the company may be able to build comparable-scale AI computing infrastructure at lower costs than American peers. ByteDance has also recently reached an agreement to purchase millions of Qualcomm chips to support its AI Agent services.

**Why it matters:** ByteDance's massive AI infrastructure spending plan directly shapes AI supply chain demand and investment theses.
