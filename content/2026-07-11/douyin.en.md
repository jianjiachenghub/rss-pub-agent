Over the next few years, AI’s scarcest resources may not be models, but chips, capital, and regulatory room to operate.

Today’s key takeaway: the AI race is evolving from “who has the strongest model” into a long-term war spanning computing supply, financing power, and industry rules.

First, SK Hynix believes the global memory shortage could last beyond 2030. Customers are already signing long-term contracts, and the company even plans to triple wafer output by 2034. This means memory is moving beyond short inventory cycles, and high HBM prices could persist for longer. But the more aggressive the expansion, the greater the risk of future oversupply.

Second, the United States is preparing to ease restrictions on advanced chip exports to the UAE. Gulf countries could regain access to high-end computing power, expanding the market for Nvidia and other chipmakers. The geography of the compute race is stretching beyond China and the US into the Middle East.

Third, Apple has formally sued OpenAI, accusing former employees of taking confidential information about unreleased products and its supply chain. The lawsuit could directly slow OpenAI’s hardware plans. It also shows that AI competition has moved beyond software models into devices, talent, and trade secrets.

So when watching the AI industry from here, don’t focus only on model leaderboards. The real winners will be those who can secure memory and chips for the long term, maintain access to low-cost capital, and protect their talent while staying within regulatory boundaries.
