Capital investment is growing, and the constraints are becoming increasingly tangible.

Host A: Let’s start with memory chips at the very top of the supply chain. SK Hynix CEO Kwak Noh-jung believes that global memory shortages could persist beyond 2030, driven by AI demand and data center investment. Some customers have already signed long-term contracts, and the company plans to triple its wafer output by 2034.

Host B: This suggests that the memory industry may be shifting away from short-term inventory cycles toward a cycle driven by long-term computing-capacity contracts. Products such as HBM are likely to retain strong pricing power in the near term, but there is another side to consider: if manufacturers bring new capacity online at the same time while demand growth slows, today’s shortage could become tomorrow’s surplus.

Host A: Capital markets have already begun pricing in this long-term story. SK Hynix completed a $26.5 billion share offering in the United States—the largest-ever initial U.S. share sale by a foreign company—and its shares rose 13% on the first day of trading.

Host B: Large-scale financing can fund capacity expansion, but after that rally, the market will also demand stronger profits and cash returns. Similar pressure is emerging in the bond market. As tech giants borrow heavily to build AI infrastructure, some investors have begun selling longer-dated AI-related bonds. In other words, the market still believes in demand for computing capacity, but it is unwilling to shoulder excessively long payback periods without conditions.

Host A: The geographic boundaries of chip demand are also shifting. The United States plans to ease restrictions on advanced technology exports to the United Arab Emirates, opening the door for the country to purchase high-end semiconductors.

Host B: If the policy takes effect, it will expand both the Gulf data center market and the addressable market for suppliers such as Nvidia. But easing restrictions does not mean shipments will begin immediately. Technology-transfer rules, end-use reviews, and specific licenses will still determine the scale and pace of procurement.

Host A: Now let’s look at models and product entry points. OpenAI has released the GPT-5.6 family, with Sol, Terra, and Luna covering flagship, balanced, and lower-cost needs, respectively. Sol’s Ultra mode can also coordinate four AI agents by default to handle complex tasks in parallel.

Host B: Even more notably, Chat, ChatGPT Work, and Codex have been integrated into a single desktop application. Users can move seamlessly between Q&A, office work, and development. The focus of competition is therefore no longer just “which model is stronger,” but also who can control the unified workspace—and whether multi-agent collaboration can genuinely save time and money.

Host A: OpenAI’s hardware plans, however, are facing legal uncertainty. Apple has sued OpenAI in the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of California, alleging that former employees took confidential information about unreleased products, components, and supply chains. Apple is seeking an order to stop the information from being used, destroy the materials, and even redesign products that have yet to launch.

Host B: The key issue in this case is not simply the conduct of individual employees, but how the court defines trade secrets and whether it issues an injunction. If the case moves into discovery or forces a redesign, OpenAI’s device supply-chain decisions and launch timeline could both be pushed back. Reports that more than four hundred former Apple employees have joined OpenAI may also prompt tech companies to reassess hiring firewalls and how quickly access privileges are revoked when employees leave.

Host A: On content licensing, Meta has paused a feature that used material from public Instagram accounts to generate images. Its opt-out design had drawn criticism from talent agencies and others, and Meta acknowledged that the product had serious design flaws.

Host B: The message here is clear: just because content is public does not automatically mean a platform has permission to use it for generative features. In the future, platforms may need clearer licensing, opt-out, and source-review mechanisms. That will increase launch times and content costs, but it could also reduce disputes over creators’ rights.

Host A: There is also an open-source project worth watching in the tech community. pgrust is attempting to reimplement PostgreSQL in Rust and says it has passed the original Postgres regression test suite in full.

Host B: Passing those tests means it has crossed an important threshold in semantic compatibility, but it still has a long way to go before it can serve as a production replacement. Extension support, operational tooling, performance, and long-term upgrade costs are what database users will actually evaluate. For now, it is better understood as a substantial engineering proof of concept than as the arrival of a mature alternative.

As the ceasefire agreement begins to fray, risks to energy transportation and financial sanctions are both rising.

Meanwhile, inflation in Brazil cooled more than expected, creating room for another interest-rate cut. U.S. markets, for their part, are entering earnings season, when lofty valuations will ultimately have to be justified by revenue, margins, and returns on capital.
