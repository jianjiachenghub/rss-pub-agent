The most important takeaway today is this: AI has moved from the “storytelling phase” into the “show me the numbers phase.”

Whether it’s company valuations, job shifts, or development costs, the market no longer cares only about who makes the loudest claims. It now wants to know whether AI is actually embedded in real workflows, whether it improves efficiency, and whether it delivers controllable returns.

The first signal: someone at MIT Sloan has built a real enterprise AI adoption index, measuring whether companies are actually using models in customer service, R&D, operations, and decision-making. This means that in the future, judging a company’s AI depth won’t be about launch events or strategic slogans. It will be about how deeply its workflows have been transformed.

The second signal: the employment impact has already spread to Ireland’s tech sector. In the past, multinational tech companies cared about taxes and talent costs. Now, if AI can replace part of operations, support, and junior technical roles, the logic of location choice becomes: where can people plus machines achieve the highest efficiency?

The third signal: AI costs are also starting to spiral. SemiAnalysis mentioned that some teams’ token spending has already reached the equivalent of 30% of employee salaries. After Uber rolled out Claude Code, it even burned through its full-year budget early at one point.

So today’s conclusion is very clear: AI is no longer just an innovation label. It is becoming a new means of production for companies. The real gap from here will not be about who has connected to AI, but who can use AI deeply, calculate its value clearly, and keep it under control.
