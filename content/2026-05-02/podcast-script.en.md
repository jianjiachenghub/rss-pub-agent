**Podcast Script: Personal Daily Selects**

---

**[Opening]**

**A:** There's a pretty contradictory picture in the market today—the S&P 500 just posted its longest weekly winning streak since late last year, and Wall Street's fast money is trading at fever pitch. But on the flip side, Buffett's Berkshire Hathaway is sitting on three hundred and eighty billion dollars in cash, net selling stocks for the fourteenth consecutive quarter.

**B:** That's basically the snapshot of today's market: risk appetite looks high on the surface, but smart money is lying low. Today we're focusing on a few hardcore shifts happening in the AI industry against this complex backdrop, plus some macro signals worth watching.

---

**[Main Thread One: AI Moving from Virtual to Physical]**

**A:** Exactly. AI isn't just about cloud-based large language models anymore. Look at Meta—they just acquired a robotics AI company, didn't disclose the name or the amount, but clearly they want AI to grow hands and feet, to actually enter the physical world and get to work.

**B:** Meanwhile, the hardware layer is accelerating too. Cerebras, that company specializing in large-scale AI computing chips, is reportedly filing for IPO at a four-billion-dollar valuation. What does this tell us? AI infrastructure and commercialization is shifting from concept hype to competing on hard capabilities. Capital is starting to price actual execution ability.

---

**[Main Thread Two: The Geopoliticization of Regulation]**

**A:** But before you compete on capabilities, you've got to get past regulation. One key piece of news—China blocked Meta's acquisition of the VR glove company Manus.

**B:** This shatters the illusion of Singapore as an "AI safe harbor." Meta wanted to fill gaps in their XR ecosystem, but got stopped by geopolitical factors. Going forward, for these kinds of critical tech M&A deals, jurisdiction of incorporation doesn't matter anymore—geopolitics is the core variable.

---

**[Main Thread Three: New Variables in Technical Approach]**

**A:** There are also shifts on the technical side. DeepSeek previously released a multimodal model technical report on GitHub, proposing a "visual primitives" framework, claiming to solve spatial reference problems—getting AI to truly understand reference relationships in physical space. They deleted it later, but the thinking is crucial.

**B:** Right. Multimodal competition has entered deep waters now. Whoever solves spatial reasoning will score big in robotics, autonomous driving, and similar scenarios. This could change how we evaluate model sophistication going forward.

---

**[Main Thread Four: The Brutal Reality of Commercial Execution]**

**A:** Speaking of execution, here's a cautionary tale. That nuclear-powered AI company Fermi was highly anticipated—they had land and power lined up to supply AI data centers—but ended up failing without signing a single customer.

**B:** That's pretty realistic. AI infrastructure isn't guaranteed just because you have the tech and resources. Closing the commercial loop is much harder than the technical vision itself. This exactly mirrors what's happening at Apple—Mac Mini inventory got wiped out because AI development demand is so intense, pushing prices up to seven hundred and ninety-nine dollars. Supply and demand is still the hard rule. No matter how hot the concept is, delivery capability is what actually counts.

---

**[Side Thread One: Boundaries of Social Rules]**

**A:** There's another boundary issue worth noting. A Chinese court just ruled that companies can't use "introducing AI" as a justification for layoffs. This draws a legal red line for AI applications in human resource management.

**B:** Exactly. As technology advances, social acceptance and legal frameworks have to keep up. AI can improve efficiency, but it can't be a direct excuse to replace human workers. This precedent will have major implications.

---

**[Side Thread Two: Divergence in Macro Policy]**

**A:** Finally, let's look at the macro environment. The European Central Bank is now leaning toward hiking rates to fight inflation, but the Fed is still at a crossroads—the Financial Times specifically discussed whether they'll adjust inflation targets or policy tools.

**B:** This will create a divergence in global capital costs. If the ECB tightens while the Fed stays loose, capital flows get messy. Plus, correlations between emerging markets are strengthening, so traditional diversification is losing effectiveness. Asset allocation is getting harder.

---

**[Closing]**

**A:** So you see, even though Buffett is hoarding cash, stocks keep climbing. The Financial Times had a good analysis—investors are now weighting expectations for long-term tech revolutions higher than traditional geopolitical risks.

**B:** As long as the moat is deep enough and growth certainty is strong enough, macro instability just becomes background noise. That might be why Wall Street's fast money and Buffett's caution can coexist. That's it for today's selection. See you tomorrow.
