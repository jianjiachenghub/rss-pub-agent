The most important takeaway today: AI competition is no longer just about building better models. It’s reshaping capital, jobs, and business workflows.

The clearest signal is money. Tech giants have doubled their debt to $350 billion within five years to build AI data centers. The compute race is shifting from burning cash to taking on leverage. Future winners won’t be determined by model performance alone, but also by interest rates, cash flow, and data center utilization.

The second shift: AI is lowering the barriers to entrepreneurship while increasing job uncertainty. The U.S. is seeing a new wave of business creation. Meanwhile, for the first time in decades, China has not set a target for new urban jobs over the next five years. Policy may be shifting from “how many jobs are created” toward skills transformation, job quality, and social protection coverage.

Third, companies are discovering that more tools don’t necessarily make better agents. GitHub revealed that Copilot’s code reviews actually got worse after more powerful tools were added. Performance improved only after simplifying the interfaces and strengthening the evidence chain.

So in the second half of the AI race, the truly scarce resources may not be models, but the capacity to absorb capital costs, govern workflows, and reorganize how humans and machines work together.
