**Podcast Script: Today's Picks from *Personal Daily***

**Duration:** Approximately 4 minutes 50 seconds  
**Roles:** Host A (Male voice, analytical), Host B (Female voice, humanities-focused)

---

**[Opening]**

**A:** Hey, have you noticed? Today's tech news has a subtle vibe. We used to say AI was "the hope of the entire village," but today's headline is—Apple has finally entered the game, yet the market is dumping AI stocks instead. Feels like the morning after the party?

**B:** Right, it's that moment when the dream is big, but the bill is brutal. Today's through-line is actually crystal clear: the AI industry is standing at a crossroads. On one side, tech giants are desperately cramming AI into operating systems and super-apps; on the other, capital markets and the real economy are starting to seriously crunch the numbers. Today let's talk about this process of "bubble-bursting."

---

**[Item One: Apple AI]**

**A:** Let's start with Apple. They released "Apple Intelligence," and a new Siri is coming. This is basically Apple officially admitting: yeah, the old Siri wasn't great, so now we're starting from scratch.

**B:** (Laughs) Right, and Bloomberg leaked that Apple went through endless internal wrestling matches over this new Siri. Think about it—a company famous for its closed ecosystem now has to deeply integrate AI into the system. This isn't just a technical issue; it's organizational surgery.

**A:** Exactly. The pressure Apple faces now is—when AI becomes infrastructure, they can't keep being that perfectionist who's always half a beat behind. But the question is, will consumers actually pay for these features? Or will it just drain their batteries faster?

---

**[Item Two: OpenAI Redesign]**

**B:** Speaking of commercialization anxiety, OpenAI is making bigger moves. ChatGPT is supposedly getting its biggest redesign ever, transforming from a simple chatbot into a "super-app," integrating coding tools and AI agents.

**A:** This shift is fascinating. OpenAI used to be about making tools; now they're clearly trying to become a platform, directly targeting developers and the enterprise market. I noticed they're also pushing an "Economic Research Exchange Program," systematically studying AI's impact on employment.

**B:** That's kind of like a preemptive move to defend themselves? (Pause) But seriously, when OpenAI starts telling enterprise stories and even preparing for an IPO, its valuation logic shifts from "how many users" to "how many programmers can it replace." For ordinary workers, this shift might be more worrying than the technology itself.

---

**[Item Three: Chip Supply Chain]**

**A:** Tech can't land without hardware. Today there's a key signal: Google was revealed to have ordered millions of AI chips from Intel. This isn't just a big order—it shows Google is actively diversifying its supply chain, not putting all eggs in the Nvidia basket.

**B:** And this is a lifeline for Intel. Their foundry business (IDM 2.0) hasn't been looking good, so this Google client gives them crucial validation. The stock jumped immediately, showing the market still buys the logic of "manufacturing diversification."

**A:** Right, the chip war is shifting from "design hegemony" to "manufacturing hegemony." When everyone has design capabilities, the key becomes who can actually manufacture, manufacture cheaply, and manufacture reliably.

---

**[Item Four: Market Sentiment]**

**B:** But the market doesn't seem fully buying this optimism. South Korea's stock market crashed today; Samsung and SK Hynix, these chip giants, got hammered because investors are doubting the growth prospects of AI demand.

**A:** This is a delicate signal. On one hand, Nvidia is still signing cooperation agreements with Korean companies to push AI infrastructure; on the other hand, investors are voting with their feet, feeling that previous expectations for AI chips were too inflated. This tension between ideals and reality is exactly the "de-bubbling" process we're seeing.

**B:** Like you said, overestimating demand? Or rather, everyone finally realizing that not every company needs to train large models, and the inference market explosion might not come that quickly.

---

**[Item Five: Macro Pressure]**

**A:** Speaking of pressure, the macro picture isn't calm either. Analysts at Citadel and Charles Schwab are warning the Fed might raise rates sooner than expected. Employment data is too hot; inflation won't stay down.

**B:** This creates a "double squeeze" on tech stocks. Today we saw AI sectors selling off while oil prices surge, and capital is fleeing risky assets. JPMorgan traders even downgraded their short-term market outlook.

**A:** Right, when risk-free rates rise, those "future cash flow" stories aren't so sexy anymore. The AI industry now has to prove it's not just an expensive toy, but a tool that actually saves money.

---

**[Item Six: Enterprise Costs]**

**B:** Speaking of the contradiction between saving money and spending it, there's a particularly grounded observation today: as AI costs climb, some companies are starting to strictly scrutinize employee travel expenses.

**A:** (Laughs) That's so real. CEOs are discovering that buying AI tool licenses for everyone, plus computing costs—the bills are scarier than imagined. So they have to pinch pennies from coffee budgets and travel budgets. What does this tell us? The ROI on AI hasn't reached the point where companies are willing to happily cut other expenses for it.

**B:** That's actually a healthy signal. When CFOs start asking "Is this money well spent?" the industry will move from the demo phase toward becoming a real productivity tool.

---

**[Item Seven: Social Response]**

**A:** Finally, something a bit warmer. UK Labour leader Starmer announced they'll roll out new tools at national job centers to help workers cope with AI-driven employment shocks.

**B:** This is an interesting policy signal. Before, AI unemployment was just academics talking; now governments are deploying specific buffer measures. Not banning technology, but helping people transition.

**A:** Right, when technology races ahead, society needs these "brake pads" or "safety belts." This also reminds us that evaluating AI isn't just about chip performance and stock prices—it's also about that middle-aged person at the job center who needs to learn new skills.

---

**[Closing]**

**B:** So you see, today's news pieces together a complete picture: tech giants transforming amidst anxiety, supply chains seeking balance through restructuring, capital becoming picky amid sobriety, and society preparing to bear the costs.

**A:** Exactly. AI's honeymoon is over; the trivial, mundane realities of married life are unfolding. This might be when the industry truly matures.

**B:** Alright, that's *Personal Daily* for today. Stay observant, stay skeptical. See you tomorrow.

**A:** See you tomorrow.

**[Outro Music]**
