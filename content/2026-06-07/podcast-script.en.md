**Personal Daily Podcast Script | Dual-Host Dialogue Version**

**[Opening]**

**A:** Today, the AI world feels like... money is burning, and burning fast.  
**B:** Right. Whether it's Google spending over $900 million monthly on GPUs, or Meta considering issuing new shares to raise capital, it feels like everyone's shifted from "competing on who has the better tech" to "competing on who can burn more cash and afford to keep burning."  
**A:** Today, let's follow this thread of the "AI infrastructure capital war" and see exactly where the money is flowing into these key nodes.

---

**[SpaceX and Google: The Hard Signal on GPUs]**

**A:** First, let's talk about this SpaceX and Google agreement. $920 million per month, lasting over three years, just to lock in the computing power of 110,000 Nvidia GPUs.  
**B:** Those are hard numbers. The key is that this is seen as a "hard signal" of GPU supply tightness—Google would rather pay this huge premium to buy from SpaceX than wait for market prices to cool down, which shows the computing power gap really can't be filled in the short term.  
**A:** And the agreement includes performance clauses—if SpaceX can't deliver by September next year, Google has the right to directly lower the price or terminate the deal. No wiggle room whatsoever.

---

**[Meta: The Equity Financing Dilemma]**

**A:** On one side, Google is making massive purchases. On the other side, Meta is in a bit of a bind. They're considering issuing new shares to raise tens of billions for AI infrastructure, and the stock price dropped immediately on the news.  
**B:** Zuckerberg himself admits he doesn't have a "very precise plan" for how AI products will make money. This is awkward—capital expenditure is supposed to double to around $140 billion by 2026, but revenue is uncertain.  
**A:** What's more telling is choosing equity financing over issuing debt. It shows debt pressure is already unbearable, and they have no choice but to dilute existing shareholders to maintain cash flow. That's not a good signal.

---

**[Talent Movement: Deep Competition in Chip Design]**

**B:** Speaking of infrastructure, talent is also moving quietly. Clive Chan, an early member of OpenAI's chip team, jumped to competitor Anthropic this week.  
**A:** This is crucial. AI competition has deepened from "buying cards" to the level of "designing chips themselves." Anthropic poaching architecture talent suggests they're likely accelerating their self-developed hardware layout.  
**B:** Meanwhile, OpenAI and Broadcom's 10GW custom chip project was originally planned for delivery in the second half of 2026. Now that the core architect has left, whether they can maintain that technical roadmap is really uncertain.

---

**[Data Centers: The Shovel Sellers' Valuation Frenzy]**

**A:** The infrastructure side is also going crazy. Data center developer Switch is negotiating a new round of financing, targeting a valuation of $50 billion—that's exactly five times its valuation when it went private in 2022.  
**B:** Asset management giants like Brookfield and KKR are all fighting for a piece. This validates an underlying logic: no matter how the AI applications above compete, the "shovel sellers"—data centers—always make steady money.  
**A:** And if Switch's financing succeeds, they might relaunch their IPO next year. SoftBank tried to acquire them before but failed. This time the valuation is rising so fast, it really reflects strong demand on the client side.

---

**[Microsoft pg_durable: The Technical Inflection Point of Compute Pushing Down]**

**B:** On the technical side, there's also a development worth noting. Microsoft open-sourced a project called pg_durable, a PostgreSQL extension written in Rust.  
**A:** Simply put, it allows databases to not just store data, but also execute business logic directly inside, avoiding the network round trips between the application layer and the storage layer.  
**B:** This might change the design thinking around microservices architecture. If compute really pushes down to the storage layer, the future system entry point might not be the application server, but the database itself.

---

**[Qualcomm and Automotive AI: Real-World Deployment]**

**A:** Application scenarios are also advancing. Qualcomm just announced their automotive AI ecosystem, the Claw initiative, collaborating with vendors like ThunderSoft, Desay SV, and Thundercomm to deploy large models directly into cars.  
**B:** This is substantial progress for on-device AI. Without needing to query the cloud for everything, the vehicle's onboard system itself has agent capabilities, which is good for both real-time performance and privacy.

---

**[Open Source Tool "Zhifan": Reflections on Vibe Coding]**

**A:** Finally, let's mention an interesting open-source project called "Zhifan" (Returning to Awareness). It targets the currently popular vibe coding—that kind of programming where you just go with the AI's flow, mindlessly clicking "continue."  
**B:** This tool can explain the logic behind every code change the AI makes, preventing you from finishing a project without knowing what you actually wrote. I think this is quite important—it reminds us that no matter how strong AI is, internalizing knowledge still depends on ourselves. We can't hand everything over to a black box.

---

**[Closing]**

**A:** So looking at today, AI infrastructure has indeed entered a war of attrition, with everything from chips to data centers being repriced.  
**B:** But the tension between technology and business is also becoming apparent—on one side, sky-high investments and talent poaching; on the other, people are seriously thinking about ROI, monetization paths, and programmers' own growth.
