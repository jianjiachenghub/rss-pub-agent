B: Right. On one side, Google, Meta, and the open-source community are all turning Agents into more complete toolchains. On the other side, institutions like the IMF, the Bank of England, and BlackRock are starting to warn that the debt, valuation divergence, and automated trading risks behind the AI boom may enter market pricing sooner than we think.

A: Let's start with the tools layer. Google open-sourced `agents-cli`, a Python command-line tool that lets developers create, evaluate, and deploy AI Agents on Google Cloud. It has already received nearly 3,800 GitHub stars.

B: The signal is pretty clear: Agent development is no longer just about writing a chatbot. It's moving into lifecycle management. Putting creation, evaluation, and deployment into one CLI means Google Cloud wants to connect Agent workloads to its own runtime and release pipeline. In the future, teams evaluating Agents will look more like teams evaluating an online service.

A: Meta is also making moves. It open-sourced `astryx`, an AI Agent design system written in TypeScript. Its focus is not the model itself, but the interface layer of Agent applications.

B: That's important. Agent products are moving beyond a single chat box toward reusable components for task status, tool calls, conversation flows, and result presentation. For companies building Agent applications, the cost is not just model calls. It's also interaction design and product engineering. The emergence of a design system shows that this format is starting to standardize.

A: On the open-source model side, DeepReinforce released Ornith-1, based on Qwen 3.5 and trained with reinforcement learning. It focuses on coding Agents and reached 82.4% on SWE-bench.

B: Competition in coding Agents is shifting from "can it write code" to "can it reliably solve real problems in real repositories." It also comes in multiple sizes, from 9B to 397B MoE, with an MIT license. That will further lower the barrier for teams to experiment with coding Agents.

A: Now let's look at capital markets. IMF official Adrian warned that compared with AI stock valuations, the debt issuance around AI investment deserves more attention.

B: What that means is that when markets watch AI risk, they can't just stare at price-to-earnings ratios. Data centers, chips, and model training all require massive capital expenditure. If more of that depends on debt, then credit spreads and debt-servicing capacity may reflect pressure earlier than stock prices do.

A: BlackRock has also incorporated AI divergence into asset allocation. It lowered its view on emerging market equities because of AI-related risks, while becoming more positive on short- and medium-term eurozone government bonds.

B: This shows that AI is not distributing its benefits evenly. Whoever has computing power, platforms, and the ability to absorb capital spending is more likely to keep being priced favorably by investors. Conversely, if emerging markets lack those supports, their weight in global equity allocation could be pushed lower.

A: But short-term market risk appetite is still there. The Dow broke above 52,000 for the first time, the S&P and Nasdaq also rebounded, and U.S. stocks recorded one of their strongest quarterly performances since 2020.

B: Capital has not simply pulled out of tech stocks. Instead, it is spreading again between core economic stocks and growth sectors. The key question next is whether corporate earnings can cover interest-rate uncertainty. Retail investors are also reinforcing this structure. Citadel Securities observed that retail dip-buying this year has been happening at a record pace.

A: Credit markets are also hot. In the first half of the year, international bond issuance from emerging markets reached 450 billion dollars, a record for the period, as issuers took advantage of spreads near their narrowest levels in almost 20 years.

B: That shows investors are still willing to take emerging market credit risk. But the narrower the spreads, the thinner the risk compensation. If the dollar or U.S. Treasury yields rebound, the refinancing window could tighten very quickly. This also connects with HSBC's warning about a dollar rebound. The external interest-rate environment remains the core variable.

A: At the macro policy level, the European Central Bank is also making markets a bit harder to read. Governing Council member Rehn mentioned that the energy shock is creating stagflationary effects, meaning growth pressure and inflation pressure could appear at the same time.

B: So pricing European assets will become more complex. Markets need to judge the pace of rate cuts while also guarding against inflation stickiness caused by energy prices. At the same time, in the U.S., there is uncertainty around Federal Reserve personnel and policy, including Trump-related controversies, which has brought central bank independence back into market focus.

A: Coming back to AI risk, Bank of England official Breeden warned that the use of AI Agents in financial markets could create market disorder through linked automated decisions.

B: This risk is not about a single model making a mistake. It's about many Agents acting at the same time on similar signals. For example, risk control, trading, and hedging models could all automatically adjust positions, amplifying feedback loops. In the future, when financial institutions evaluate AI, they won't just look at accuracy. They'll also need to look at how systems influence one another.

A: Companies are also starting to feel cost pressure. The Financial Times noted that corporate AI spending may exceed budgets, as model calls, computing power, and integration needs are all pushing up bills.

B: This can be viewed together with the financing news around Moonshot AI's Kimi. Kimi's pre-money valuation in its new round rose to 31.5 billion dollars, ARR surpassed 300 million dollars, and API revenue accounted for more than 70%. The valuation anchor for large model companies is shifting from parameters and traffic toward revenue quality, API share, and pricing power.

A: Supply is also tight. Because of a global shortage in cloud computing capacity, Google restricted Meta's access to Gemini's top-tier model. Meta had originally used it for fraud detection and harmful content filtering.

B: This shows that top-tier model capability is beginning to show signs of rationing. Models are not just products. They are strategic resources. Who can get access to computing power and models will directly affect foundational capabilities like content moderation, safety, and fraud prevention.

A: Finally, let's look at industry diffusion. Tokyo and Seoul continue to increase investment in AI construction, and China's industrial robots are also entering more use cases with the help of AI. AI investment is not just software. It is moving into infrastructure, manufacturing, and robotics.

B: So today's main thread can be summed up in one sentence: AI is moving from a technology theme to a balance sheet theme. Developers are getting more complete Agent tools, companies are taking on higher costs, and markets are starting to reassess debt, interest rates, regional divergence, and automation risk.

A: That also means that when we watch AI next, we shouldn't only look at which company released a new model. We also need to look at who can deploy it, who can pay for it, who can finance it, and which balance sheet ultimately carries the risk.
