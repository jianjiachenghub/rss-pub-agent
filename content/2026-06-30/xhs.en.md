Title: A Lot to Take In Today

Body:

Today’s focus is not just AI, but “AI is now affecting developer tools, financial markets, asset allocation, and industrial investment.”

I’ve compressed the most important things worth reading from June 30 into a few key directions:

1. AI Agents Are Entering the Engineering Stage

Google open-sourced `agents-cli`, which can be used to create, evaluate, and deploy AI Agents on Google Cloud.

Meta also open-sourced `astryx`, which componentizes the UI for Agent applications.

This shows that Agents are no longer just “chatbox toys.” They are moving toward a full product workflow: development, evaluation, deployment, interface, and tool use are all starting to become standardized.

2. Open-Source Coding Agents Keep Heating Up

Ornith-1 was open-sourced. Built on Qwen 3.5 + RL, it reached 82.4% on SWE-bench.

The point of these models is not simply that they can write code, but that they can handle tasks, fix bugs, and run workflows more like engineers. The coding Agent race is still accelerating.

3. AI Risk Is Shifting From Valuations to Debt and Leverage

IMF officials warned that the real concern around AI may not be expensive stocks, but the debt financing behind them.

Data centers, chips, and model training all burn a lot of money. If more and more capital expenditure is supported by borrowing, market stress may show up first in credit spreads rather than stock prices.

4. Large Institutions Are Reallocating Assets Based on AI Exposure

BlackRock downgraded emerging-market equities, with AI-driven market divergence being one of the reasons.

Put simply: whoever has compute power, platforms, and the ability to absorb capital expenditure is more likely to attract capital. Markets that fail to capture the AI dividend may see their weight reduced in global allocations.

5. Risk Appetite Has Returned to U.S. Stocks, but It Is Not a Blind Rally

The Dow broke above 52,000 for the first time, while the S&P 500 and Nasdaq also rebounded sharply. The S&P and Nasdaq also recorded their best quarterly performance since 2020.

But the key questions are: can corporate earnings support high valuations, and can interest-rate uncertainty be absorbed?

6. Retail Investors Are Changing the Rhythm of Market Pullbacks

Citadel Securities observed that retail investors have been buying dips at a record pace this year.

This means that when markets fall, retail investors and passive funds may help support prices together, making pullbacks shorter and shallower, while also making it harder for institutions to judge hedging timing.

7. Corporate AI Costs Are Starting to Get Out of Control

The Financial Times noted that corporate AI spending may exceed budgets.

Model calls, compute, integration, and internal system upgrades all add to costs. For many companies, the question now is not “whether to use AI,” but “how to control the AI bill.”

8. AI Investment in China and East Asia Continues to Diverge

On one side, China’s stock market is seen as not having fully benefited from the global AI boom. On the other, Tokyo and Seoul continue to make large-scale bets on AI infrastructure.

Meanwhile, China’s industrial robots are using AI to expand into new scenarios, showing that the industrial side is not inactive. It is just that capital-market feedback and industrial implementation are not moving in sync.

One-sentence summary for today:

AI is shifting from a technology hotspot into a shared variable for capital markets, industrial investment, corporate budgets, and financial stability. What matters next is not “who released a new model,” but who can truly turn AI into revenue, efficiency, and sustainable cash flow.
