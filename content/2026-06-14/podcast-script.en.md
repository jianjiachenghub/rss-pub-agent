**Podcast Script: Personal Daily Digest**

**[Opening]**

**A**: Today we're first zeroing in on a specific timeline marker—it's been exactly 100 days since the conflict broke out in Iran.

**B**: 100 days... This is indeed a rather interesting observation window. Usually, we might assume that when there's a war, central banks have to do something, right? Cut rates, or at least make a statement. But this time, both the Fed and the Bank of England are holding their ground.

**A**: Right, both sides are sitting tight, not rushing to adjust rates just because of external conflicts. What does this tell us? Compared to short-term geopolitical fluctuations, inflation is still what's giving them the bigger headache.

**B**: Exactly, the logic has shifted. The supply shocks brought by war could actually push prices higher. At this point, blindly easing would be like pouring gasoline on the fire. So you see, even as the conflict continues, policy focus remains stuck on that balancing point between inflation and growth, unmoved.

---

**[Transition]**

**A**: This "unmoved" high-rate environment has actually given rise to other opportunities. Speaking of which, there's a pretty specialized financial product that's suddenly heating up lately—CLO ETFs.

**B**: CLOs, the ETF version of Collateralized Loan Obligations. They used to be mainly institutions' game, but now retail investors can participate through ETFs too.

**A**: Right, fund flows have been particularly noticeable. The logic is simple: with rates staying high and liquidity tight in the private debt market, everyone urgently needs tools that can hedge against inflation while remaining liquid.

**B**: CLOs happen to be floating rate—the higher rates go, the higher the coupon follows. Plus, they're way more flexible than investing directly in private debt. This is kind of investors' "defensive offense" in the current environment.

---

**[Transition]**

**B**: Moving from financial markets to tech. Domestic GPU manufacturer Moore Threads recently made some pretty key progress—they completed Day-0 adaptation for the MiniMax M3 large language model.

**A**: Day-0 adaptation, this term matters a lot now. It means the moment the model is open-sourced, they can get it running that same day, without a lengthy migration and debugging period.

**B**: Right, this reflects full-stack engineering capabilities. From model adaptation to deployment to optimization, they can now keep pace with the first tier. People used to worry that domestic GPUs couldn't run mainstream large models; now that concern can be partially laid to rest.

**A**: This isn't just a technical issue, it's an ecosystem issue. Only by achieving this kind of immediate response can they gradually break dependence on CUDA and lower migration costs. The coordination between domestic computing power and leading large models has entered a new phase.

---

**[Closing]**

**A**: So these three threads today—macro policy resolve, financial market adaptation, and breakthroughs at the technical foundation—are actually all telling the same story: whether facing war or technological blockades, the system is finding its own rhythm of response.

**B**: Exactly. Sometimes observers need more patience than participants. See you next time.
