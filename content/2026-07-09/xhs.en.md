Today, tech and finance are focused on one thing: AI is no longer just a product upgrade. It has started to affect chips, energy, interest rates, and capital markets.

**Title: AI Has Rewritten the Market Narrative**

The key areas worth watching today:

1. **AI assistants are entering the “getting work done” phase**  
OpenAI is continuing to push ChatGPT toward becoming a work partner, not just a chatbot. It can work across apps and files, break down tasks, and move long-term projects forward. GPT-Live voice has also been upgraded so it can listen and speak at the same time, translate in real time, and handle pauses and background noise.  
The point is not that it is “more human,” but that the AI entry point may expand from a text box into meetings, commutes, customer service, learning, and in-car scenarios.

2. **AI supply chain pressure is spreading from GPUs to memory, power, and financing**  
Micron raised its U.S. investment plan to $250 billion, mainly because AI is driving demand for memory. Meta is also pushing forward with its own AI chips and long-term supply agreements.  
This shows that AI infrastructure is not just short of GPUs. HBM, storage, electricity, and locked-in supply chains are all becoming new variables.

3. **AI capital spending is starting to affect macro policy**  
The New York Fed president noted that AI demand could become a source of inflationary pressure. If demand for data centers, power, equipment, and labor keeps rising, AI will be more than a growth story. It will also affect expectations for rate cuts.  
Put simply: the more money AI burns, the more markets have to recalculate interest rates and valuations.

4. **Energy and geopolitical risks are heating up again**  
Russia’s diesel exports are constrained, making global diesel supply tighter. The conflict between the U.S. and Iran is escalating, and Middle East risks continue to weigh on market sentiment.  
If energy prices rise further, they will directly affect inflation, shipping, and manufacturing costs, while also making central banks more cautious.

5. **The financialization of AI is becoming more obvious**  
A $35 billion AI chip credit deal led by Apollo has started to circulate, and BlackRock also believes AI capital spending will continue to support the investment theme over the next two to three years.  
But it is worth noting that AI infrastructure risk is spreading from tech company balance sheets into credit markets and asset management portfolios.

6. **New shifts are emerging in China AI and cross-border capital**  
China may tighten cross-border controls on some domestic AI models, while European and U.S. investment banks are playing a smaller role in financing Chinese AI companies.  
This means AI is not just a technology race. It is increasingly shaped by regulation, capital channels, and geopolitics.

One-sentence summary for today:  
**The AI narrative is still alive, but markets are no longer just asking “how powerful are the models?” They are asking who pays for the compute, where the electricity comes from, how long the financing can last, and whether it will push inflation higher.**
