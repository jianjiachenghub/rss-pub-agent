The most important takeaway today is this: AI is no longer just a tech-stock story. It is becoming a macro variable, starting to affect inflation, interest rates, energy, and credit markets.

Why?

First, Fed officials have already put AI demand on their inflation watchlist. AI capital spending drives up demand for chips, equipment, electricity, and high-end labor. If that demand keeps going, rate-cut expectations could be repriced.

Second, AI supply-chain bottlenecks are spreading from GPUs to memory and energy. Micron has raised its U.S. investment to $250 billion. Meta is still developing its own AI chips and signing long-term supply deals. The logic behind all of this is the same: the compute expansion is not over, and memory, power, and manufacturing capacity are all being repriced.

Third, AI financing is changing too. A $35 billion AI chip credit deal led by Apollo has started trading, showing that AI infrastructure risk is moving off tech company balance sheets and into private credit and asset-management portfolios.

The application layer is accelerating as well. OpenAI has launched a stronger work-focused ChatGPT and real-time voice capabilities, while Apple is pushing on-device large models onto the iPhone.

So the core issue today is not “what new model did AI release?” It is this: AI is shifting from product innovation into a full repricing of capital spending, energy demand, and interest-rate expectations. What markets will watch next is not just model capability, but who can withstand the costs, financing pressure, and supply-chain strain.
