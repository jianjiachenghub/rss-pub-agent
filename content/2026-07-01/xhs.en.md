Not Just AI: What’s Most Worth Reading Today

The core message of this July 1 daily report is simple: AI is still accelerating, but the market has started asking, “Where does the money come from, where are the returns, and who bears the risk?”

1. AI models: Anthropic is moving fast

Anthropic released Claude Sonnet 5. The key point is not that it is “yet another new model,” but that it brings near-flagship Agent capabilities to a cheaper tier.

That means browser operation, terminal execution, coding, and automated tasks may not always need to run on the most expensive models in the future. For developers and AI Agent products, unit costs could fall, and the room to scale could expand.

At the same time, the U.S. has lifted some export restrictions on Anthropic models, and Claude is now fully available on Microsoft Azure. The signal behind this is clear: advanced models are moving from a stage of “capability competition” into “compliant distribution + enterprise procurement.”

2. AI infrastructure: Beyond GPUs, power and debt are now on the table

Oracle warned that massive investment in AI data centers may not necessarily earn back the expected returns.

Meta is preparing to sell idle AI computing capacity, the UK’s National Grid is investing in a U.S. AI power company, and AI financing is also starting to enter the private debt market.

This shows that competition in AI infrastructure is not just about “who has more GPUs,” but also about:

Compute utilization  
Long-term customer contracts  
Power supply  
Financing costs  
Data center cash flow  

If the AI boom continues, power and debt may become the next round of keywords.

3. Financial markets: AI stocks are hot, but leverage risks are heating up too

AI stocks in South Korea and Taiwan continue to attract capital, and South Korean exports are also being supported by demand for AI chips.

But it is worth noting that some of the gains are being driven by debt and high-risk trading. In other words, the chip cycle is real, and so is crowded trading.

If financing costs rise and margin pressure increases, volatility could be amplified.

4. Crypto and the dollar: Risk appetite is cooling

Bitcoin has fallen to a 21-month low and has also broken below its 200-week moving average.

Technical levels like this can affect market confidence, and capital may refocus on U.S. dollar rates, leverage liquidations, and macro risks.

At the same time, the dollar is strengthening, and emerging-market currencies have erased their gains for the year. Even strong AI stocks may not be enough to fully offset the pressure from the dollar and interest rates.

5. Interest rates: Markets are starting to fear “rate hikes” again

Some investors in the U.S. market are already betting on the risk of a July rate hike. The European Central Bank has not fully closed the door on further hikes, and the Bank of England has also said rate cuts are still some way off.

This means the market is no longer only discussing “when cuts will come,” but is once again pricing in the possibility of “more hikes.”

For tech stocks, growth stocks, and emerging-market assets, this is a sensitive variable.

6. Jobs and industry structure: AI’s impact is already showing up in the data

The U.S. tech and financial sectors are losing about 28,000 jobs per month.

It is still hard to conclude whether AI is the only reason, but companies have already begun using AI to reassess roles, outsourcing, efficiency, and labor costs.

The more practical change is this: AI is not only helping companies add new capabilities, it is also changing the structure of white-collar jobs.

Today’s most important thread:

AI capabilities continue to improve, but the market is starting to care more about costs, compliance, compute returns, and infrastructure bottlenecks. Meanwhile, interest rates, the dollar, leverage, and employment pressure are reminding everyone that while the technology narrative is strong, the macro environment is not easy.
