A: The biggest story to watch today is the way two forces are simultaneously shaping the market. On one side, AI investment continues to expand, with spending increasing across chips, memory, and devices. On the other, the escalating conflict in the Middle East is once again putting pressure on energy prices, inflation, and global risk appetite.

B: And these two themes are intersecting and influencing where money flows. Investors need to assess both how long the AI boom can last and where oil prices, interest rates, and the dollar are headed.

A: Let’s start with AI hardware. TSMC’s latest quarterly sales rose 36% year over year, reaching the high end of market expectations. That suggests demand for AI computing chips remains resilient.

B: Intel is also planning to invest €5 billion in its manufacturing hub in Ireland. The key issue now is no longer just advanced process nodes—high-bandwidth memory and advanced packaging are equally scarce. AI servers have changed the memory industry’s traditionally standardized, highly cyclical business model, with profits increasingly concentrated in high-performance products. South Korea, meanwhile, plans to invest the additional tax revenue generated by the AI industry in future growth projects, attempting to turn the current chip windfall into long-term competitiveness.

A: However, the way companies think about using AI models is changing. More and more businesses are adopting Chinese models, mainly not because they want larger parameter counts, but because they want to reduce deployment and inference costs.

B: This means companies are placing greater emphasis on return on investment when choosing models. At the same time, the United States is discussing restrictions on Chinese developers using American models for so-called “adversarial distillation.” In the future, technology controls could extend beyond chips, computing power, and model weights to cover API outputs and training practices. For model service providers, customer screening, usage monitoring, and cross-border compliance will all become more complicated.

A: China’s AI industry itself is also entering a new policy and product cycle. Xi Jinping will attend China’s flagship artificial intelligence summit for the first time, signaling that AI is receiving attention at a higher level of government.

B: There are also new developments on the device side. StepFun has launched the STEPX Neo agentic smartphone. The focus is not on adding yet another chatbot, but on giving the AI agent system-level permissions and enabling it to complete services and transactions across apps. Its partnerships with platforms such as Trip.com and Alipay are worth watching. If it can only answer questions, there will be little differentiation. But if it can reliably complete tasks such as booking tickets and making payments, the criteria for judging smartphone competition will change.

A: Apple is taking a different route. The company is adjusting its Mac chip strategy and redirecting talent and technology from its discontinued self-driving car project toward AI.

B: We’re also seeing rotation in the capital markets. As AI-related stocks came under pressure, money moved into Apple, adding roughly $650 billion to its market value. For now, Apple has become a safe haven within the technology sector. However, Apollo chief economist Torsten Slok warns that if AI assets undergo a significant correction and US assets become less attractive, the dollar could also come under pressure.

A: Now let’s turn to today’s other major story—the situation in the Middle East. The United States has resumed its blockade of Iranian vessels passing through the Strait of Hormuz and has proposed charging a fee equal to 20% of the value of cargo transported through the route by others. Fears of supply disruptions have already pushed oil above $80 a barrel, while US stocks have fallen in response.

B: The biggest questions right now are whether the strait can remain open to normal shipping and how the fees would be enforced. Meanwhile, following US military strikes, Iran has attacked Bahrain, Kuwait, and Qatar, spreading the conflict to several Gulf countries. If high oil prices persist, markets may reprice inflation and expectations for interest-rate cuts, while the aviation, transportation, and chemical industries will face higher costs.

A: And the risks extend beyond the energy market. A CLO fund has fallen 50% in value, triggering a power struggle among the parties involved. Pressure from asset disposals and pricing in private credit is beginning to spill over into public markets.

B: Other regions are also building up financial buffers. India is urging state-owned banks to attract more foreign-currency deposits to strengthen foreign-exchange liquidity. On the geopolitical front, Moscow has faced a second consecutive day of intense Ukrainian drone attacks, as long-range strike capabilities continue to reshape the boundaries of offense and defense for both Russia and Ukraine.

A: Finally, here’s a tool for developers to keep an eye on. Graphify can turn code, SQL, documents, and even directories of images and videos into structured, searchable knowledge.

B: This reflects a new direction for coding assistants. The challenge is shifting from “help me write a piece of code” to “first, understand this large project.” If its indexing is accurate and it integrates well with tools such as Claude Code and Codex, it could reduce the cost of searching complex repositories and help new team members get up to speed more quickly.

A: To sum up today, AI spending remains well supported, but companies are paying more attention to costs and real-world delivery. Meanwhile, the conflict in the Middle East has brought energy, inflation, and interest-rate risks back to the center of the market.

B: Going forward, there are three key variables to watch: the actual shipping conditions in the Strait of Hormuz, whether oil can remain above $80 a barrel, and whether AI hardware orders can continue translating into revenue across the supply chain. That’s all for today’s Personal Daily Brief.
