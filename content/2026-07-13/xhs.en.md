# Today’s Biggest Story Isn’t Just AI

The main threads on July 13 are clear: AI investment remains red-hot, but developments in the Middle East, oil prices, and financial risks are once again shaping markets.

📌 Demand for AI chips remains strong

TSMC’s quarterly revenue rose 36% year over year, while Intel plans to invest €5 billion in its Irish plant. This suggests that spending on AI computing infrastructure has yet to cool significantly, with advanced process nodes, advanced packaging, and high-bandwidth memory remaining key areas across the supply chain.

However, capital is already rotating within the tech sector: as AI stocks came under selling pressure, Apple emerged as a “safe haven,” adding roughly $650 billion in market value.

📌 When businesses choose AI, value for money matters more

More companies are adopting Chinese AI models for a practical reason: lower deployment and inference costs. Competition is shifting from “who has the most parameters” to “who can solve real-world problems for less.”

Meanwhile, the U.S. may extend technology restrictions beyond chips, computing power, and model weights to cover API outputs and model distillation. Cross-border model access could face significantly higher customer-screening and compliance costs in the future.

📌 AI phones are beginning to compete for control of the system layer

StepFun has launched STEPX Neo, featuring a system-level AI agent and integrations with services including Trip.com and Alipay.

What really matters is not whether it can hold a conversation, but whether it can gain enough system access to complete real tasks across apps, such as booking tickets and making payments. Smartphone competition may be moving from “app assistants” into the era of “agent operating systems.”

📌 The Middle East is once again a key market variable

The U.S.-Iran conflict has spread to Bahrain, Kuwait, and Qatar, while the actual navigability of the Strait of Hormuz remains disputed. Oil prices have risen above $80, and shipping faces insurance costs, military risks, and potential additional fees.

If high oil prices persist, inflation could rebound and expectations for interest-rate cuts may shift. Cost pressures in aviation, transportation, and chemicals deserve particular attention.

📌 Two more warning signs are emerging in financial markets

A CLO fund plunged 50%, suggesting that stress is beginning to spread from private credit into public markets. An Apollo economist also warned that a sharp correction in U.S. AI assets could weigh on the dollar.

India, meanwhile, is encouraging state-owned banks to increase foreign-currency deposits, building an early buffer for foreign-exchange liquidity.

📌 International developments cannot be ignored either

Moscow faced a second consecutive day of intensive Ukrainian drone attacks, as long-range drones continue to reshape the boundaries of offense and defense in the Russia-Ukraine war.

South Korea is preparing to invest additional tax revenue generated by the AI industry into future-growth projects, seeking to turn the short-term chip boom into long-term industrial capabilities.

Three storylines are worth watching today:

1. Whether the Strait of Hormuz can remain open  
2. Whether AI capital expenditure can continue supporting the chip supply chain  
3. Whether the rotation in tech stocks will develop into a broader repricing of risk
