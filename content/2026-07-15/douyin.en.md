If you still think AI is just a software upgrade, you may be looking at it backward: the biggest shift today is that capital, computing power, and risk are all concentrating in AI infrastructure.

First, the industry side. Demand for AI chips continues to surge, prompting lithography giant ASML to raise its outlook. Meanwhile, CXMT plans to raise around $10 billion—another sign that memory-chip expansion is accelerating.

But this capital shift is already creating losers. IBM warned that customer budgets are moving from traditional software to AI hardware and memory chips. Its stock plunged more than 25% in a single day, wiping out $69 billion in market value. AI isn’t just creating new growth—it’s also repricing the old giants.

There’s another crucial signal from China: Apple Intelligence for the Chinese market has finally completed regulatory filing and will integrate Alibaba’s Qwen. The question now isn’t whether it can launch, but when—and how much system-level traffic Qwen will gain access to.

But the stronger the capabilities, the more critical the data boundaries. Grok Build CLI was reportedly found silently uploading entire code repositories, prompting xAI to urgently shut down the feature.

In one sentence: AI competition is moving beyond model parameters into an all-out battle over chips, capital, distribution, and security.
