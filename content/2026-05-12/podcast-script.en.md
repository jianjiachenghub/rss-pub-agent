**B:** Speaking of tech, there's an interesting comparison I wanted to share. Anthropic recently released a security analysis model called Mythos, claiming it's exceptionally good at finding code vulnerabilities—so good they didn't dare release it casually.

**A:** And the results?

**B:** The author of curl—that command-line HTTP tool—actually tested it. Scanned 176,000 lines of code. Guess what? Found just one low-severity vulnerability.

**A:** If you did a manual audit, or if Claude just read through it, you'd probably get similar results?

**B:** Basically, for battle-tested open-source projects like curl, Mythos was mostly just "rediscovering what's already known." This says a lot—AI in security auditing is still far from replacing professional developers. There's a real gap between the marketing claims and actual capabilities.

**[Transition]**

**A:** But on the flip side, AI-assisted development is actually proving its worth. There was this developer living in a city with terrible noise, kept getting woken up at night but couldn't find the culprit.

**B:** He used AI to solve it?

**A:** Yeah, spent about eight hours total. From using phone recordings to collect noise data, to analyzing spectral patterns, to generating complete code for a tracking system—AI assisted throughout. Finally pinpointed it to specific noises from upstairs during certain time periods.

**B:** I love this case. It's not one of those "we're going to change the world" grand statements—just solving a very specific pain point in daily life. Trading eight hours for peaceful sleep afterwards. Very practical.

**A:** Exactly, and it shows that current AI toolchains can really lower the barrier to development. You don't need to be a full-stack expert; if you have an idea, you can rapidly implement it.
