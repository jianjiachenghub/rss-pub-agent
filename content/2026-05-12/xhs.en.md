Alright, let's talk about two pieces of news worth noting in both the tech and finance circles today.

The first one: inflation data is soaring again! The U.S. CPI for April rose to 3.8% year-over-year, hitting a three-year high. The main reason for this sudden jump in inflation might surprise you—it’s actually linked to the conflict in the Middle East. Due to rising tensions, oil prices have surged, pushing up gasoline prices in the U.S. This ripple effect from energy prices has ultimately impacted what we commonly refer to as inflation data. Following the release of these figures, market expectations for a Federal Reserve interest rate cut have cooled again. In simple terms, borrowing costs might rise, which could put pressure on the valuations of tech stocks and emerging market assets.

However, in the long run, if underlying issues like energy transition and supply chain adjustments aren’t resolved, geopolitical-driven price fluctuations like this may continue to appear from time to time.

The second piece of news is about AI model safety. Anthropic (the company behind Claude) recently introduced a model specifically designed to evaluate AI safety, called Mythos. They claim it’s exceptionally adept at identifying code security vulnerabilities—so much so that they’re hesitant to make it fully public for now. But here’s the interesting part: the creator of curl (the widely-used networking tool) recently used Mythos to check curl’s own 170,000 lines of source code. Guess what happened? Mythos only found 11 low-risk vulnerabilities. This result seems at odds with Anthropic’s earlier claims of “exceptional excellence,” highlighting the gap that can exist between the hype around large AI models and their real-world performance. It also suggests that more third-party, practical testing is still needed.

Finally, here’s an interesting little story. A developer living in a noisy city, constantly disturbed by sounds while trying to sleep, used various AI programming tools (like Claude 3.5 Sonnet, Cursor, and v0) to build a personal sleep noise tracking system—in just about 8 hours. From idea to finished product, it was incredibly fast. This story is quite inspiring because it shows how today’s AI tools are genuinely lowering the barrier to “bringing ideas to life,” empowering individual developers to quickly tackle cool, personalized projects.
