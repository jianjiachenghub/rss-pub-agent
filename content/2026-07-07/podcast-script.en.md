A: The main thread worth watching today is that AI competition is shifting from “whose model is stronger” to “who can secure enough stable and affordable compute.”

B: Right, and that theme is especially concentrated today. First, DeepSeek was reported to be developing its own AI chips, with the goal of reducing reliance on external high-end accelerators. That signal is important. If model companies move into chips, the competition is no longer just about parameters, performance, and product experience. It also includes inference costs, supply chain stability, and whether software and hardware can work together effectively.

A: Along the same logic, several other pieces are about Chinese companies reducing their dependence on Nvidia and adopting more domestic AI chips. This does not necessarily mean a single chip’s performance will immediately lead across the board. It means enterprise procurement priorities have changed: whether chips can be bought continuously, deployed at scale, and whether they will be affected by policy. These factors are becoming more important.

B: This also gives local AI chip companies more certain demand. But two practical questions still matter: whether ecosystem adaptation is smooth enough, and whether cluster stability is good enough. AI infrastructure does not end with buying a batch of cards. It is a combined problem of long-term operations, framework compatibility, scheduling efficiency, and cost accounting.

A: Overseas, the scale of AI investment is also continuing to expand. Amazon plans to issue at least $25 billion in bonds to support AI infrastructure spending. In other words, AI is no longer just part of tech companies’ R&D budgets. It is now affecting credit markets.

B: The bigger backdrop is that AI capital expenditure by Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, Microsoft, and Oracle is expected to exceed $800 billion by 2026, and could reach $1.1 trillion in 2027. At this level, it has already become a macro variable. The market’s next question will be: can these investments ultimately turn into measurable revenue and profit, rather than staying only at the infrastructure construction stage?

A: Some parts of the supply chain are already benefiting first. Samsung posted a new profit high for the third consecutive quarter, mainly driven by AI demand, with demand for high-end memory and related semiconductors still being released. This shows that AI capital spending is not only flowing to cloud and chips. It is also pulling along memory, equipment, and the broader semiconductor chain.

B: Still, financial market sentiment around AI is heating up again and again. There was also news today that tech giants drove the stock market higher, and the AI trade became active again. In the short term, capital has returned to large tech stocks. But whether that can continue still depends on profit realization, and on when all that capital spending we mentioned earlier can actually pay back.

A: Beyond the market, there are also new changes on the regulatory side. Major banking regulators issued warnings about AI-driven cyberattacks, meaning the attacks financial institutions face will become more automated and more complex. While AI improves efficiency, it is also increasing attackers’ capabilities. Bank security budgets and risk control processes may both need to be adjusted.

B: On the macro side, a New York Fed survey showed that U.S. consumers’ short- and medium-term inflation expectations rose in June, especially for medical care and rent. This will affect the Federal Reserve’s policy patience. If housing and medical expectations rise again, the market’s bets on rate cuts will need more confirmation from subsequent inflation data.

A: Another change in capital flows: venture capital is pouring into defense technology. Aerospace and military-related startups are no longer being avoided by Silicon Valley the way they used to be, and investment has already doubled. Capital preferences are shifting from pure software growth toward policy orders, geopolitical security, and hard-tech capabilities.

B: At the corporate strategy level, Tencent sold $1.5 billion worth of Kuaishou shares, which has been interpreted as accelerating its shift toward AI. This reflects a change in asset allocation by major companies: reducing stakes in mature internet platforms and moving resources toward cloud, models, and content ecosystems, to see whether these can form new growth curves.

A: Finally, there is also a signal from developer tools. OfficeCLI on GitHub has gained attention. It is an open-source C# tool that allows AI agents to read, edit, and automate Word, Excel, and PowerPoint without installing Microsoft Office.

B: That is interesting, because the entry point for AI agents is not only in IDEs or chat windows. It is also beginning to enter the office documents most commonly used by enterprises. The key to future knowledge workflows may not be whether AI can write a piece of text, but whether it can directly take over document reading and writing, formatting, and cross-file collaboration.

A: To sum up, today’s main thread is that AI is entering a heavier phase: chips, bonds, capital expenditure, regulation, and office automation are all being pulled in.

B: Yes. The three variables worth watching next are: whether domestic compute can be deployed at scale and with stability, whether overseas giants’ AI spending can generate sufficient returns, and how security and regulation will keep up after AI moves from tools into enterprise workflows.
