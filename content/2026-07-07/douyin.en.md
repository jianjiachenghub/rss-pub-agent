The most important takeaway today is this: AI competition is shifting from “whose model is stronger” to “who controls compute, capital, and supply chains.”

One clear signal: DeepSeek is reportedly developing its own AI chips. At the same time, Chinese companies are moving more aggressively toward domestic AI chips, reducing reliance on Nvidia’s high-end accelerators. The key here is not just performance. It is whether the chips can be secured reliably, deployed at scale, and used to bring inference costs down.

The second signal comes from U.S. tech giants. Amazon plans to issue at least $25 billion in bonds to keep investing in AI infrastructure, while the five biggest tech companies could spend more than $800 billion on AI capital expenditure in 2026, possibly approaching the scale of U.S. military spending. AI is no longer just a normal industry investment. It is now affecting credit markets, GDP, and capital cycles.

The third signal is the repricing of capital. Samsung has posted repeated profit highs on AI demand, and AI themes are once again pushing tech stocks higher. Tencent’s sale of Kuaishou shares is also being read as a move to redirect more resources toward AI.

So today’s core conclusion is simple: in the second half of AI, the battle is not just about model parameters. It is about chips, cash flow, supply chains, and commercial returns. Whoever can turn massive investment into real revenue is the one most likely to win in the end.
