Today’s must-read: AI is starting to affect your wallet

There’s a lot to take in today. This is no longer just “AI got another upgrade.” More importantly, AI is moving from tech news into a shared variable across prices, stocks, jobs, regulation, and geopolitics.

1. AI models keep getting stronger, but regulation is tightening too

OpenAI previewed GPT-5.6 Sol, highlighting its capabilities in coding, science, and cybersecurity. At the same time, the U.S. government was reportedly asking OpenAI to release new models in phases, with user reviews before broader access.

This shows that top-tier models are no longer products that can simply be released once they’re ready. They are now moving according to a regulatory rhythm. In the future, model competition won’t only be about capability, but also about who can pass compliance faster and win enterprise trust.

2. AI costs are starting to reach ordinary consumers

Apple has raised prices for Macs and iPads due to memory shortages. The starting price of the MacBook Air rose from 8,499 yuan to 9,999 yuan, and the iPad Pro also saw a clear price increase.

The logic behind this is: AI data centers are taking up large amounts of memory and computing resources, pushing up hardware costs, which may eventually become price pressure when consumers buy computers and tablets.

So AI is not just a story about cloud giants. It is already starting to affect your electronics budget.

3. Tech stocks no longer rise just because they are “AI-related”

South Korea’s stock market fell more than 8%, and Asian tech stocks were also hit. The market is starting to worry: AI investment is too expensive, and can end-user demand absorb it?

At the same time, one study says AI revenue has covered depreciation costs for the first time, suggesting AI capital spending may be becoming more economically viable.

Taken together, these two things are interesting: the market has not stopped believing in AI. It has become more selective. Going forward, investors will care more about who can truly turn AI into revenue, profit, and cash flow.

4. Memory chips are becoming a key battleground

Samsung and SK Hynix are reportedly preparing to significantly increase AI-related investment. AI is driving demand for HBM and DRAM, and the logic of the memory industry is shifting from traditional cycles toward long-term AI capital spending.

But questions are also emerging: Will rapid capacity expansion hurt prices? Is demand sustainable enough? This will directly affect Korean tech stocks and the global hardware supply chain.

5. AI companies are entering the IPO countdown

OpenAI is reportedly considering an IPO as early as 2027, and Anthropic is also seen as potentially listing even sooner.

This means leading AI companies will move from the private market, where they “sell the future,” into the public market, where financial statements matter. Revenue growth, computing costs, loss scale, and governance structures will all be repriced.

6. AI assistants are shifting from personal tools to team members

Anthropic released Claude Tag, upgrading Claude Code into a more enterprise-collaboration-oriented tool. It enters Slack and supports shared context, persistent memory, proactive intervention, and asynchronous execution.

This direction is important: AI is no longer just a chat box. It is starting to live inside team workflows. Enterprises will care more about permissions, memory isolation, token budgets, and task auditing.

7. Macro conditions and risk assets are tightening too

U.S. core inflation expectations have been raised, and Fed rate cuts may continue to be delayed. After Bitcoin’s sharp drop, the market is still debating whether it has bottomed, but many believe it has not yet reached a true panic-buying point.

In short: before liquidity clearly loosens, high-valuation tech stocks, crypto assets, and growth companies will be more vulnerable to pressure.

8. Geopolitics is also pushing up costs

China’s key minerals are putting pressure on Japan, and future passage through the Strait of Hormuz may become fee-based. These factors could affect semiconductors, batteries, energy, shipping, and inflation expectations.

The risks in tech supply chains are no longer just about chip manufacturing. They also include upstream minerals, shipping routes, and policy approvals.

Today’s key takeaway:

The AI theme is still intact, but the market has moved from the “imagination stage” into the “do the math stage.”

The four questions most worth watching next:

Can AI costs be accepted by consumers?

Can AI companies generate enough revenue to cover computing power and depreciation?

Will tech stocks shift from broad gains to sharp divergence?

Will regulation change the release rhythm of top-tier models?

Today’s keyword is not “AI explosion,” but this: AI is starting to enter the real world’s cost sheets, price lists, and regulatory timelines.
