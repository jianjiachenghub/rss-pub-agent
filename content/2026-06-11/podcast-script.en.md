**A**: There's a really noticeable feeling today—AI is completing its identity shift from "laboratory tool" to "national infrastructure."

**B**: Exactly, and this process is colliding with geopolitical turbulence. Let's look at the broadest picture first: The European Central Bank has directly raised interest rate and inflation expectations due to the Iran situation.

**A**: This is worth paying attention to. We used to think geopolitical conflicts were far removed from central banks, but now we're seeing the transmission chain between energy, shipping, and inflation is incredibly short. Market expectations have shifted from "manageable friction" to risks that "could escalate at any moment."

**B**: Speaking of the national level, multiple countries are pushing for AI sovereign wealth funds, treating artificial intelligence as a strategic asset on par with oil and telecommunications.

**A**: Yes, "state-owned AI" is no longer just a concept—it's happening through sovereign funds and state-owned enterprises making direct investments. This is the complete opposite of the laissez-faire model of the internet era; technological sovereignty has become Priority One.

**B**: There are also milestones in the capital markets. Altman confirmed that OpenAI plans to IPO within a year, which will establish a valuation anchor for the entire AI sector.

**A**: The impact will be profound. The investment pace in primary markets, exit paths for startups—even comparing it to SpaceX's IPO, which was historically the hardest to price—are all redefining how tech stocks are played.

**B**: Back to corporate moves, Anthropic's launch of Claude Corps is quite interesting. They're not donating money; instead, they're hiring 1,000 young people to work full-time at non-profits while paying them salaries and providing AI tools.

**A**: This is essentially turning "AI for all" into a strategic asset. It tests model deployment in public service scenarios while laying groundwork for government partnerships—it's not just brand PR.

**B**: On the technology implementation side, Agents are moving from demos to serious products. OpenAI acquired Ona to build long-running enterprise agents, the Apache Foundation incubated Burr to manage agent state, and Amazon released EvalKit for system evaluation.

**A**: This shows the industry realizes that building a working agent and building an enterprise-grade agent that runs stably for three months are completely different things. Engineering tools are filling the gap between prototype and product.

**B**: Data from Amazon's Bedrock team confirms this too. Six people completed in 76 days what would have taken 30 people a year, but they emphasize this isn't "AI-assisted programming"—it's "AI-native development"—humans do the orchestration, AI is the core.

**A**: But there's a pressure signal on the cost side. TSMC's CFO made it clear that due to inflation driving up operating costs, they haven't ruled out raising prices for chip manufacturing.

**B**: This is the pricing cornerstone of global technology. If TSMC raises prices, costs will pass through everything from NVIDIA GPUs to iPhones, potentially changing the cost structure of AI compute deployment and even affecting inflation expectations.

**A**: Finally, let's mention changes in primary markets. ETF products targeting unlisted giants like SpaceX have emerged, compressing the space for "hot money" that traditionally relied on arbitraging information gaps around IPOs.

**B**: Capital is looking for earlier entry points. This "pre-IPO democratization" represents a structural shift for the entire venture capital ecosystem.

**A**: So today's main thread is clear: AI is embedding itself into the underlying structures of national capital, physical industries, and geopolitics.

**B**: The boundaries between technology, wealth, and power are getting increasingly blurred. This might be the new normal we need to get used to.
