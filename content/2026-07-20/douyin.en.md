Don’t just focus on whose model ranks higher. The biggest shift today is this: AI competition is moving beyond models—and into computing power, costs, and business returns.

First, China’s AI foundation is getting stronger. Z.AI has completed a large-scale training center powered entirely by domestically produced chips. The real test is no longer whether the chips exist, but whether large-scale training can run reliably, how efficient it is, and how much software adaptation will cost.

Second, Chinese models are disrupting the existing pricing structure. Kimi K3 has drawn attention from Silicon Valley and investors, while Alibaba’s shares rose as much as 5.4% intraday after it released a new model. The market is starting to reassess: if capabilities are approaching the leaders, do they still justify the same high premium?

Third, it’s time to scrutinize the AI bill. BlackRock plans to raise more than $12 billion to finance Meta’s data centers. Meanwhile, executives at multiple companies are being pressed on the token cost of each call—and how much revenue those calls actually generate.

In one sentence: the first half of the AI race was about who was stronger. The second half is about who can reliably turn that capability into revenue—at a lower cost.
