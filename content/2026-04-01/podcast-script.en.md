The rich get valuations pumped higher and higher, while those without money might not even get a ticket to entry. But looking at it the other way, with all this money, is the service guaranteed to be stable?

This is actually a warning for enterprise users: the stability of AI services currently still falls short of traditional cloud service standards.

**Host A:** So enterprises need to have a realization now: they can't just blindly believe in large models. They need multi-model redundancy and have to guard against single points of failure. Speaking of failures, that Anthropic incident yesterday was what you’d call a truly "epic" blunder.

**Host B:** You mean the Claude Code source code leak?

**Host A:** Exactly. Because an NPM package mistakenly included a source map file, over 50 million lines of source code were left completely exposed. Everyone suddenly realized there were anti-distillation strategies inside, and even an "undercover mode" capable of hiding the AI's identity.

**Host B:** That’s actually pretty awkward. What was originally a moat-like architectural design has now been fully exposed to competitors, effectively making part of the technical barrier "open-sourced." For those trying to catch up, this saves a lot of effort in reverse engineering.

**Host A:** This also brings up another topic: governance in the AI era. Now that Agents are so unruly, traditional static management simply can't keep them under control. Over at AWS, they proposed an AIRI framework specifically to handle the risks brought by this kind of dynamic interaction.

**Host B:** That's right, and there's the engineering level too. A principal engineer at Thoughtworks also suggested that the commands used to manage AI assistants should be treated as infrastructure. Don't just rely on individual "prompting skills"; turn it into a team engineering asset, so code quality doesn't fluctuate wildly.

**Host A:** Moving on from tech, let's zoom out a bit. On the geopolitical front, The Economist mentioned that the "Trump hurricane" could push China off its original course and toward alternative alliances.

**Host B:** This means the pressure for tech decoupling is escalating. For tech companies relying on global supply chains, compliance costs and market uncertainty need to be recalculated, and they need to prepare backup plans in advance.

**Host A:** The situation in the US isn't optimistic either. The so-called "Year of Liberation" hasn't truly freed American factories; simply relaxing regulations won't solve labor and structural issues. Investors looking at manufacturing reshoring need to be wary of the disconnect between policy slogans and actual costs.
