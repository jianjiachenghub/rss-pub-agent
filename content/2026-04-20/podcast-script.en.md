**Podcast Script: Personal Daily**

**[Opening]**

**A:** For today's episode, we need to set the tone first—look, on one side you've got tech giants in an intense race over AI computing power, and on the other, geopolitical clouds are weighing heavily on the market. When these two threads intertwine, they form the central axis of investment logic for the period ahead.

**B:** Right, essentially we're finding certainty in the gap between the technological singularity and great power rivalry. Today we'll dig into a few threads worth thinking through.

---

**[Main Thread 1: The Computing Power War Heats Up]**

**A:** Let's start with tech. Reports surfaced that Google is developing new AI chips, directly targeting Nvidia. On the surface this looks like Google wanting to reduce dependence on Nvidia's H100 and B200, but deeper down, even Microsoft and Meta are doing in-house designs—what does this tell us? The moat for AI infrastructure is shifting from "who has the cards" to "who can build the cards."

**B:** And the math doesn't add up. Nvidia's margins are so high that while cloud vendors face heavy upfront investment in manufacturing their own chips, economies of scale should bring costs down eventually. Though this might not hurt Nvidia short-term—demand is still there—their long-term monopoly is definitely loosening.

---

**[Main Thread 2: The AI Macro Paradox]**

**A:** Speaking of long-term impact, Northern Trust, managing $1.4 trillion, came out with a pretty counterintuitive take today—they said the AI boom might bring "large-scale deflationary effects."

**B:** That's an interesting angle. The traditional narrative says AI drives up electricity demand, drives up capex, so inflation stays sticky. But Northern Trust's logic is: if AI really triggers a productivity revolution, supply-side efficiency gains might outpace demand expansion, actually suppressing prices.

**A:** Exactly, this directly challenges central banks' rate-hiking logic. If this plays out, central banks might pivot to easing much earlier than we expected.

---

**[Main Thread 3: Geopolitical Risks Enter the Stage]**

**B:** But easing requires stability, and today's geopolitical picture isn't calm. Trump announced the Iran ceasefire agreement is unlikely to be extended, which pressured US stocks and sent oil prices jumping.

**A:** And the transmission was fast. Canadian inflation jumped straight to 2.4% directly because of oil prices—just when the Bank of Canada was breathing easier, they have to tense up again. Worse, the Bank of Canada's survey shows the war is already distorting inflation expectations for businesses and consumers—once this psychological anchoring sets in, the cost of unwinding it is high.

**B:** The ECB also warned that the economic fallout from the Iran war hasn't fully materialized yet. This lag means we might only be halfway down the slope, not at the bottom yet.

---

**[Main Thread 4: The Two-Way Flow of Chinese Technology]**

**A:** Shift focus to the East. The Economist had an observation worth pondering today—the world needs Chinese technology, but China has decided to keep the tech domestic.

**B:** Right, from rare earths to AI algorithms, export controls are tightening. Plus that news today about Chinese robots finishing a half-marathon in 50 minutes—this shows China actually has real capabilities at the cutting-edge application layer. We used to guard against tech outflows; now the tables have turned.

**A:** Washington's panicking too—look at USA Rare Earth announcing a $2.8 billion acquisition of a Brazilian rare earth mine today, aiming to "de-risk" critical minerals. But this might trigger new resource nationalism, fragmenting global supply chains even further.

---

**[Main Thread 5: Fragility of AI Infrastructure]**

**B:** Supply chain risks also show up at the micro level. Data center upstart Fermi collapsed today—after losing Amazon's $150 million investment, stock tanked and executives fled en masse.

**A:** That's a stark reminder. The AI infrastructure track looks glamorous, but startups are extremely dependent on a few big customers and capital infusions. Investors used to look at tech potential; now they probably need to check if customer orders have actual cash commitments backing them first.

**B:** In contrast, hedge funds are aggressively buying the dip. One fund dropped $2.54 billion on Bitcoin today, a record since late last year. Institutional money is looking for a hybrid of safety plus growth, or rather, voting with their feet to express concern about traditional tech infrastructure.

---

**[Closing]**

**A:** Today's picture is pretty clear—the tech revolution is accelerating, but geopolitics and regulatory frameworks are dragging behind. From Asian banks facing heightened scrutiny over Mythos AI, to Vercel being extorted for $2 million through third-party AI tool vulnerabilities, risks are shifting from single-point technical risks to systemic complexity risks.

**B:** So current investment strategies probably need to find a rebalance between "efficiency dividends from AI" and "tail risks from geopolitical friction." Let's see how the market digests these signals tomorrow.

**A:** Alright, that's it for today.

**[End]**
