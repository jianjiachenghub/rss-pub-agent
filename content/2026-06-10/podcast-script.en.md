**[Personal Daily] Podcast Script**

**Hosts**: A (Rational Perspective), B (User/Emotional Perspective)  
**Total Duration**: Approx. 4 min 30 sec  

---

**A**: Hey, have you noticed? AI seems to have suddenly jumped out of the chat box.  
**B**: What do you mean?  
**A**: Just looking at today's topics, you can feel AI drilling down into the most foundational infrastructure—from the concrete and steel of data centers to browser kernels, all the way to circuit optimization on chips. It's no longer about simply building an app, but reconstructing the entire tech stack. Let's spend a few minutes today peeling back this layer of change.

**B**: Let's start with what's hitting users most directly. Is Chrome finally cracking down hard on ad blockers?  
**A**: Right. Legacy tools like uBlock Origin could still run using some workarounds before, but now Google has sealed even the last backdoor. Essentially, this is a head-on conflict between the platform and users over data control.  
**B**: So ordinary users either watch ads or switch browsers?  
**A**: Basically those are the two options. Either migrate to browsers like Firefox that still support legacy extensions, or accept the limited blocking features built into Chrome. For users who rely on privacy protection tools, this is indeed a hurdle they have to face.

**A**: On another front, what DeepSeek is doing is even more interesting. They're hiring "civil engineering bros"—literally construction engineers.  
**B**: An AI company hiring civil engineers? To build office buildings?  
**A**: Way more hardcore than that. They're building self-owned gigawatt-scale data centers—we're talking million-kilowatt capacity. What's that concept? It's equivalent to the power consumption of a small city.  
**B**: So they used to rent from others, but now they're personally getting into building facilities?  
**A**: Exactly. This signals the AI computing infrastructure race has entered hand-to-hand combat. When model training requires ten-thousand-card clusters, power, cooling, and network architecture—these "heavy assets"—become core competitive advantages. Renting isn't as good as building; they're trying to keep their lifeline in their own hands.

**B**: There's also new movement on the domestic policy front. Regarding this MIIT "AI + Communications" plan, where do you think the focus is?  
**A**: The key is this term "intelligent computing network system." It's not just about letting AI use the communications network, but turning the communications network itself into nodes for intelligent computing.  
**B**: Sounds a bit abstract?  
**A**: Simply put, in the future, 5G base stations and edge data centers won't just transmit data; they'll process AI computing on-site. This is especially critical for autonomous driving, industrial internet—scenarios requiring millisecond-level response. The state is pushing computing power to the edge, avoiding everyone crowding into a few large centers.

**A**: Speaking of computing optimization, AWS dropped a major announcement today. They're using AI agents to automatically optimize the kernels of Trainium chips.  
**B**: Wait, AI optimizing AI chips? Is this some kind of inception?  
**A**: It really is. Low-level code that used to require top engineers to write by hand is now being iterated and tested by AI agents themselves. This rewrites the rules of the game—human costs come down, and optimization speed goes from weeks to hours.  
**B**: So chip engineers are going to lose their jobs?  
**A**: Not necessarily, but the role changes. People go from writing code to training AI to write code. This foreshadows that in the future, hardware performance limits may depend on AI's optimization capabilities, not how much hair human engineers have left.

**B**: On the product front, there are two contrasting stories today. Meituan released an AI browser called Tabbit, while on the Apple side, Craig Federighi is criticizing "AI for AI's sake."  
**A**: This contrast is quite interesting. Meituan's approach treats AI as the entry point—whatever you want to do, it automatically operates across apps for you, and it's permanently free. Clearly they want to grab the traffic gateway.  
**B**: What about Apple?  
**A**: Federighi emphasized at WWDC that AI must center on real needs; it can't be done just for showing off tech. This seems like a veiled jab at certain products treating AI as a cure-all.  
**B**: So which side are you on?  
**A**: I think these are two different paths. Meituan represents "AI-first" exploration—even if it's a bit rough, they put it out there; Apple represents "experience-first" restraint. The market needs the former to trial-and-error, and needs the latter to set the tone. But honestly, this kind of cross-app operation that Tabbit does, iOS's closed ecosystem couldn't do it even if they wanted to. This counts as an opportunity for the Android camp.

**A**: Finally, something programmers will care about. Meta rewrote the React core compiler in Rust, and it was mostly AI-written code.  
**B**: AI writing code again?  
**A**: Right. A core React member said this time it was "AI-led coding with close human guidance." Rust itself is known for safety; using AI to write system-level toolchains—this was unthinkable before. The key is this PR has already been merged into the main branch, meaning it passed Meta's code review. This marks AI programming formally advancing from writing business code into the core systems of the infrastructure layer.

**B**: Looking at it this way, today really spans everything from browser plugins to gigawatt-scale power stations—AI is reconstructing every layer.  
**A**: Right. Whether it's DeepSeek's concrete and steel or Meta's Rust code, essentially they're answering the same question: When AI becomes infrastructure, how should we redesign the digital and physical architecture of this world? That's it for today's Personal Daily. See you tomorrow.
