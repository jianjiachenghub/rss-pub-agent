Podcast Script: Personal Daily

(Duration: Approx. 5 minutes)

---

**A:** In today's episode, let's talk about the "cooling-off period" the AI industry is going through. It feels like since late last year, the whole market has shifted from "betting on every direction at once" to "counting every penny."

**B:** Yeah, the signals are unmistakable. Look at OpenAI letting go of Bill Peebles, the head of Sora, while Meta announces plans to cut 8,000 jobs. On one side, you've got star projects being scaled back; on the other, major tech firms using AI to replace human labor. And right in the middle of this, Cerebras is filing for an IPO, targeting the inference market that Nvidia has ignored.

**A:** Bottom line: the chip battlefield is segmenting, the application layer is shuffling, and capital is only flowing toward places that can make money. Let's drill down into a few of these developments.

---

**B:** Let's start with the personnel shakeup at OpenAI. The head of Sora is out, Kevin Will—the VP of Scientific AI—is leaving too, and even their research platform Prism is getting shut down.

**A:** This is essentially Altman "pruning" the organization. He's chopping off side quests like video generation that burn cash without generating revenue, and funneling all resources into coding assistant Codex and enterprise services. It shows that even big model companies now have to look at ROI—they can't burn unlimited money on tech demos anymore.

---

**A:** Meta's approach is even more direct. Starting May 20th, they're cutting 10% of staff—about 8,000 people—and management has made it clear there's more to come.

**B:** Zuckerberg explicitly stated this time that he's using AI to reconstruct the organizational structure and flatten management layers. The key point is that these layoffs are directly tied to AI capabilities—management will dynamically adjust plans based on AI development. This isn't simple cost-cutting; it's a signal that AI is replacing human organizational structures.

---

**B:** Meanwhile, the application layer is seeing hot money flying everywhere. Anysphere, Cursor's parent company, is currently raising $2 billion, with its valuation jumping from $9 billion six months ago to $50 billion now. Even Nvidia wants in on this round.

**A:** Quintupling in six months—that speed is terrifying. But what's interesting is Nvidia making this move—from the underlying chips all the way up to programming tools. It's a calculated strategy to lock in compute demand. If every programmer ends up using Cursor in the future, inference compute becomes a rigid demand, and Nvidia wants to ensure downstream applications run on their cards.

---

**A:** Speaking of making money, AI products are starting to show their true colors. The Economist reports that assistants like ChatGPT are beginning to build in recommendation engines, proactively pushing products to users.

**B:** This is "sponsored conversation." When you ask the AI to recommend a camera, it might prioritize models that paid for placement. This changes the logic of traffic entry points—advertiser budgets are migrating toward AI assistants, fundamentally undercutting the traditional search engine business model.

---

**B:** Looking back at China, it seems DeepSeek's Liang Wenfeng has "come around"—shifting from pure technological idealism toward a normal commercial company path.

**A:** What does this mean? Probably no more just open-sourcing and topping leaderboards, but instead seeking a balance between commercial monetization and funding.

**B:** Exactly. When the industry enters a cooling-off period, even the strongest technical teams have to think about survival. This is an important variable for China's large model landscape.

---

**A:** Finally, looking at a macro hedge. U.S. investors are aggressively buying defense stocks, casting aside their previous ESG concerns.

**B:** Geopolitics has become the core variable in asset allocation. With military spending surging, defense stocks have become dual vehicles for both safety and policy dividends. Connected to this, Hong Kong is announcing a new batch of key enterprises to attract tomorrow—including AI, low-altitude economy, and cross-border financial infrastructure—showing that everyone is competing for limited tech resources and security assets.

---

**B:** Today's theme is clear: The AI industry is moving from "fever" into "defervescence," from total war into trench warfare.

**A:** Exactly. Whether it's OpenAI's contraction, Meta's layoffs, or Cursor's high valuation, they're all showing that the market wants products that can land and make money, not PowerPoint presentations.

**B:** Alright, that's our core observation for today. See you next episode.
