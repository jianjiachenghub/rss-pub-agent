**Personal Daily · Today's Podcast Script**

**Cast**: Host A (Macro/Business perspective), Host B (Tech/Product perspective)

**A**: There's an interesting trend today—seems like whether we're talking macro policy or coding tools, the first thing out of everyone's mouth is "because of AI."

**B**: Right, and it's not just the application layer making waves. Look at what Kevin Warsh, the Fed chair nominee, said today—he mentioned that when setting rates going forward, they'll need to factor in the central bank's balance sheet situation. Basically, the pace of quantitative tightening will affect whether to cut rates and by how much.

**A**: Mm, so this turns monetary policy from a dual mandate of "watching inflation and employment" into a three-body problem. Markets used to just guess CPI and unemployment; now they have to watch how much debt the Fed itself still has to sell. If balance sheet runoff happens too fast, rates might have to stay higher for longer to offset the liquidity tightening.

**B**: Exactly. And this lines up perfectly with what's happening on the other side—GitHub's CTO posted something pretty candid today, saying that starting last December, AI-assisted coding traffic crushed their infrastructure, scaling jumped from the 10x they originally planned for to 30x, and they crashed twice.

**A**: So downstream is screaming "no compute," while upstream is restructuring too. SK Hynix and Samsung said today that because AI chips are hogging HBM memory so aggressively, customers are signing long-term fixed-price contracts instead of waiting for spot market fluctuations.

**B**: That means the memory sector might shift from "cyclical plays" to "semi-growth stocks"—revenue volatility will decrease, but valuation logic will have to change too. What's interesting though, is that while AI infrastructure is expanding like crazy, compliance is actually contracting.

**A**: You're talking about Goldman banning Claude in Hong Kong?

**B**: Right, plus the NDRC and Meta dynamic. On one side, US investment banks are delisting Anthropic's models from their whitelists due to "reliability and regulatory risks"; on the other, China's elevated the NDRC to primary enforcer in tech, negotiating AI compliance directly with Meta.

**A**: These two opposite moves actually say the same thing: there's no global standard for AI tool regulation anymore. Multinationals now have to slice their tech stacks by region, Hong Kong's financial center might see longer AI review cycles, and the costs for foreign AI products entering China are rising too.

**B**: But users seem to be finding ways around these restrictions. Look—Tencent Hunyuan dropped a 0.4-gig offline translation model today, 440MB, runs 33 languages on your phone without needing connectivity. Plus DeepSeek is A/B testing image recognition, moving from pure text to multimodal.

**A**: It's like a "decentralized" response—since cloud regulation and compliance are getting heavier, push capabilities down to the edge. Developers are moving this way too—that Rust-based terminal AI tool Warp hit GitHub's trending list today; people want lightweight, local, scriptable environments.

**B**: Though geopolitical uncertainty is still throwing cold water on all this compute demand. Part of the macro tightening we mentioned earlier is also because oil spiked to $117 again. Trump talked about maintaining the naval blockade on Iran, and Moscow warned the UAE today not to exit OPEC or oil prices would crash.

**A**: Right, Warsh's new rate framework might ultimately depend on oil prices too. If geopolitical conflict pushes inflation expectations up again, the Fed's trade-off game between "balance sheet and rates" gets more complicated.

**B**: So today's thread is actually pretty clear: AI is redefining infrastructure capacity and regulatory boundaries, but macro policymakers still have an "old map" in their hands—energy prices and central bank balance sheets. These two systems are squeezing against each other right now.

**A**: Exactly. Tech wants to run at 30x speed, but the macro brakes are still on. We'll see which side overheats first tomorrow.
