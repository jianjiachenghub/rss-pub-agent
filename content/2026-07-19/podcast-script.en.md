A: Today’s most important theme is that artificial intelligence is moving from a “model race” into a “compute monetization phase.” Token usage is growing rapidly, and companies are competing for inference capacity, but capital markets are already asking: With so much being invested, where are the returns?

B: This trend also extends to smartphones, robots, and the semiconductor supply chain. Meanwhile, geopolitical conflicts are increasing the risk of higher oil prices, further complicating European interest rates and global asset pricing.

A: Let’s start with a very striking number. The China Academy of Information and Communications Technology says China’s average daily Token usage rose from about 100 billion in early 2024 to 140 trillion by the end of March this year—an increase of more than a thousandfold in just over two years.

B: This means the industry’s bottleneck is shifting. People used to worry about whether models were usable. Now, the more practical questions are whether there is enough inference capacity, how efficiently it can be allocated, and how much each Token actually costs. This is especially true for agents, where a single task may trigger multiple rounds of model and tool calls, making demand more volatile than ordinary chat usage.

A: Compute capacity is even starting to resemble an asset that can be leased between companies. Anthropic has proposed renting AI data center capacity from Meta for two years in a potential deal worth up to $10 billion. Payments would be made monthly, and either side could exit early.

B: Those terms are worth examining. On the one hand, Meta could monetize resources that are temporarily underutilized. On the other, Anthropic would not have to immediately take on the burden of building capital-intensive infrastructure. But monthly payments and the option to exit also suggest that, while both sides are optimistic about demand, neither is ready to assume that growth will continue in a straight line forever.

A: Pressure from the demand side is already affecting products. Forty-eight hours after the release of Kimi K3, Moonshot AI suspended subscriptions for new consumer users because of limited compute capacity, prioritizing resources for existing users instead.

B: This may be a pricing signal. In the future, AI subscriptions may not be divided solely by “membership tier.” Ordinary Q&A, coding tasks, and agent workloads could be priced separately. Moonshot AI’s plan to separate its core subscription from Kimi Code benefits is essentially an effort to align its revenue structure more closely with actual inference costs.

A: But the market has clearly cooled. The AI-themed trade, which had previously gained a cumulative 105 percent, has retreated. Chip stocks have entered a technical bear market, and retail investors in leveraged funds have suffered even steeper losses.

B: Investors are shifting their focus from “who is spending more” to “when will that spending generate returns?” What may truly drive valuations next are cloud-provider revenue, chip utilization rates, and depreciation pressure. New generations of models can also abruptly reshape the competitive landscape. The higher the leverage, the faster the price adjustment.

A: Interestingly, while financial markets are worried about overinvestment, the industry is still warning of supply shortages. SK Group Chairman Chey Tae-won expects demand for AI semiconductors to grow by 60 to 100 percent next year, while new supply will be virtually zero.

B: So the bottleneck may not be limited to chips. It could spread to electrical wiring, undersea cables, factory buildings, and power infrastructure. The open-source project KTransformers has received more than 18,000 stars, reflecting another possible path: using heterogeneous hardware and software optimization to improve utilization of existing equipment and reduce dependence on a single GPU technology stack.

A: AI is also looking for new practical points of entry. NaviX Ultra, showcased at WAIC, can perform tasks across apps, such as finding videos. There were also around sixty full-size humanoid robots operating directly in public areas at the event.

B: This shows that the standards of competition are changing. For agent-powered smartphones, the key questions are whether permissions are secure, whether tasks can be completed reliably, and whether third-party apps are willing to integrate. For robots, the focus is on continuous operating time, failure rates, and the cost per task. Whether VLA or world models are better will ultimately have to be answered by real-world delivery data.

A: Finally, let’s look at the macro variables beyond AI. Long-range strikes between Russia and Ukraine have intensified, the conflict between the United States and Iran has caused more casualties, and hedge funds are increasing their bullish oil positions at the fastest rate in a decade.

B: If oil prices continue to rise, they could push inflation expectations higher again. Although the European Central Bank is not currently expected to raise interest rates, it is keeping open the possibility of tightening policy in September. In other words, today’s seemingly disconnected news about technology, energy, and interest rates is actually pointing to the same issue: growth requires more resources, and resource costs are returning to the center of pricing.

A: Those are today’s key stories. AI usage continues to expand, but the winners will not be determined by model capabilities alone.

B: It will also depend on where the compute comes from, how the costs are recovered, and whether products can generate genuinely stable demand. See you next time.
